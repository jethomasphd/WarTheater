# Operation Epic Fury Day 20: comprehensive OSINT intelligence sweep

**The 20th day of the US-Iran war saw its most consequential 24-hour escalation cycle yet.** Israel's unprecedented strike on Iran's South Pars gas field on March 18 triggered Iranian retaliatory attacks on energy infrastructure across four Gulf states — Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and the UAE — while three senior Iranian officials were killed in targeted strikes within 24 hours. Oil prices surged past $118/barrel, European gas jumped 35%, and the Pentagon announced the war's "largest strike package yet" while requesting $200 billion in supplemental funding from Congress. The conflict's geographic scope expanded to the Caspian Sea for the first time. This report synthesizes verified developments from March 18 evening through March 19, 2026, drawn from CENTCOM releases, major wire services, open-source intelligence platforms, and specialized trackers.

## 1. US and Israeli strikes reached new geographic and strategic thresholds

### CENTCOM and Pentagon statements (March 18-19)

**March 18 — Strait of Hormuz bunker busters:** CENTCOM announced that US forces "successfully employed multiple 5,000-pound deep penetrator munitions on hardened Iranian missile sites along Iran's coastline near the Strait of Hormuz." A US official confirmed these were **GBU-72 Advanced 5K Penetrators** — their first confirmed combat use. The targets were hardened anti-ship cruise missile bunkers. A US official told NBC News the strikes "penetrated hardened facilities" but "did not eliminate the entire inventory and the cruise missile threat remains."

**March 19 — Pentagon briefing (Hegseth and Caine):** Defense Secretary Hegseth announced that "to date, we've struck over **7,000 targets** across Iran and its military infrastructure" and pledged that March 19 would feature "the **largest strike package yet**, just like yesterday was" — approximately 20% larger than the previous day. Joint Chiefs Chairman Gen. Dan Caine confirmed the US is "continuing to attack deeper into Iranian territory" and "flying further to the east now" to hunt one-way attack drones. Caine reported **120 Iranian vessels and 44 minelayers destroyed**, up from 100+ vessels and 30+ minelayers reported on March 16. A-10C Thunderbolt IIs are "hunting and killing fast attack watercraft" in the Strait of Hormuz, while AH-64 Apaches are striking Iranian-aligned militia groups in Iraq.

### South Pars gas field strike (March 18)

The Israeli Air Force struck gas treatment facilities at **Asaluyeh** (the onshore processing hub for South Pars) and offshore infrastructure in Bushehr Province. **Four gas treatment plants** processing sour gas from phases 3, 4, 5, and 6 were hit by US and Israeli drones, per Argus Media citing Iranian state media. The governor of Asaluyeh confirmed facilities were "taken offline" to control fires. South Pars produces **730 million cubic meters/day** — 70% of Iran's domestic gas consumption — and the strike halted Iranian gas supplies to Iraq. The strike was coordinated with and approved by the Trump administration per Israeli officials and Axios, despite Trump initially claiming the US "knew nothing." Trump subsequently said Israel would not strike South Pars again but threatened that if Iran attacks Qatar, the US "will massively blow up the entirety of the South Pars Gas Field."

### Caspian Sea strikes — first ever (March 18 evening)

In a major geographic expansion, the **IDF struck Iranian Navy vessels at Bandar Anzali on the Caspian Sea coast** — approximately 1,300 km from Israel and 260 km from Tehran. Axios reported "more than five vessels" were targeted. Bandar Anzali hosts Iran's Northern Fleet headquarters and serves as a key node in the **Iran-Russia weapons supply corridor**. The fleet includes a Moudge-class frigate, a Hamzeh-class corvette, three Sina-class missile boats, and an Adjutant-class minesweeper. The IDF described it as "the first time Israeli forces have struck Iranian military assets in the Caspian Sea region" and the strike was guided by Naval Intelligence and the Military Intelligence Directorate.

### Bushehr nuclear plant incident (March 18)

A projectile struck the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant premises at approximately 15:11 GMT on March 18, hitting **a structure 350 meters from the reactor** — described as a "metrology service building." IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi confirmed the impact and called it crossing "the reddest line of all." He told Fox News the damage appeared caused by a drone and "doesn't seem to be very significant." Rosatom CEO Likhachev confirmed no casualties among Russian personnel; approximately 250 Rosatom employees and families had already been partially evacuated. **No party claimed responsibility.** Russia condemned the strike. The reactor itself sustained no damage and radiation levels remain normal.

### Three senior Iranian officials killed in 24 hours

**Ali Larijani**, Secretary of Iran's Supreme National Security Council, was killed overnight March 17 in an IDF airstrike on Tehran — the **most senior figure killed since Khamenei** on February 28. He died alongside his son Morteza Larijani, office head Alireza Bayat, and guards. **Gholamreza Soleimani**, commander of the IRGC Basij paramilitary force, was killed in the same wave of strikes. **Esmaeil Khatib**, Iran's Intelligence Minister, was killed in a separate targeted IDF strike on March 18 in Tehran — the third senior official killed in approximately 24 hours. Israeli Defense Minister Katz announced he had authorized the military to kill "any senior Iranian official" once an opportunity arises "without the need for additional approval."

### Updated casualty figures

The discrepancy between sources remains significant and warrants careful interpretation:

- **Iran Health Ministry** (as of March 19): **1,444 killed, 18,551 injured**. Victims aged 8 months to 88 years; 11 healthcare workers killed (4 physicians, 2 nurses, 3 emergency workers), 55 wounded.
- **Hengaw Organization** (5th report, March 18): **5,300 killed** in first 18 days — 511 civilians (9.6%, including 120 minors, 160 women), **4,800+ military personnel**. Strikes recorded in 178 cities across 25 provinces.
- **HRANA** (March 17): **3,114 deaths documented** — 1,354 civilians, 1,138 military, 622 unclassified. Notes military casualties are "significantly higher than reported" due to limited access.
- **ACLED** (through March 13): 1,879 US-Israeli strikes recorded (1,661 Israeli, 218 US), at least 73 intercepted. Nearly 2,000 distinct conflict events across 29 of Iran's 31 provinces.
- **US military**: **13 KIA** (confirmed), **~200 wounded** (180+ returned to duty). One additional non-hostile death in Kuwait.
- **Israel**: **18 killed** (Al Jazeera tracker), **3,730+ wounded**. March 18: elderly couple killed in Ramat Gan by cluster munition shrapnel. March 19: Thai agricultural worker killed at Moshav Adanim; Palestinian woman killed in Beit Awa, West Bank.

## 2. Iranian retaliation escalated dramatically after South Pars strike

### IRGC threats and follow-through

Hours after the South Pars strike, the IRGC issued explicit evacuation warnings naming five facilities as "direct and legitimate targets": **SAMREF** (Yanbu), **Jubail** petrochemical complex, **Al Hosn** gas field (UAE), **Mesaieed** and **Ras Laffan** (Qatar). IRGC Aerospace Commander Brig. Gen. Majid Mousavi promised "the most daring retaliatory strikes so far." The IRGC warned: "If it is repeated again, further attacks on your energy infrastructure and that of your allies will not stop until it is completely destroyed."

**Qatar — Ras Laffan struck twice in 12 hours:** Iran hit Ras Laffan Industrial City (world's largest LNG production facility, ~20% of global LNG supply) with at least one ballistic missile on March 18 evening, causing fires and "extensive damage" per QatarEnergy. A second ballistic missile strike hit early March 19. Qatar declared Iranian embassy military/security attachés persona non grata, invoked Article 51 of the UN Charter, and condemned the attack as "a dangerous escalation, a flagrant violation of its sovereignty, and a direct threat to its national security." A vessel was struck by an "unknown projectile" 4 nautical miles east of Ras Laffan on March 19, per UKMTO.

**Saudi Arabia:** Riyadh intercepted **4 ballistic missiles** and shot down **12+ drones** in eastern regions. Debris from an intercept fell near Prince Sultan Air Base — no damage reported. Four civilians were injured by debris in a Riyadh residential area. A drone struck the **SAMREF refinery in Yanbu** on the Red Sea coast — described as having "minimal impact" but significant because Yanbu is currently "the only export outlet for crude oil out of Gulf Arab countries." Saudi FM Prince Faisal bin Farhan said the kingdom "reserved the right to take military action" against Iran — the strongest language from Riyadh since the war began.

**Kuwait:** Drones targeted units at **Mina al-Ahmadi and Mina Abdullah refineries**, causing fires at both. Kuwait arrested 10-14 Kuwaiti nationals and 2 Lebanese affiliated with Hezbollah for plotting "terrorist actions against vital infrastructure."

**UAE:** Gas operations at Abu Dhabi's **Habshan facilities** (near Al Hosn target) were temporarily suspended after intercepted missile debris fell on the site. An Iranian projectile struck near **Al Minhad Air Base** hosting Australian troops; Australian PM Albanese confirmed "minor damage" to accommodation and medical facilities, no casualties. Dubai intercepted all inbound threats with no injuries. Cumulative UAE data (as of March 17): Iran had fired **314 ballistic missiles, 15 cruise missiles, and 1,672 drones** at the UAE since the war began.

### Hezbollah launched largest barrage yet

Hezbollah fired **more than 100 rockets in a single barrage** on March 18 evening, targeting northern, central, and southern Israel simultaneously — the **first Hezbollah rocket attack to reach southern Israel** since the war began. The barrage was synchronized with Iranian ballistic missile salvos. Israel killed more than 20 people across Lebanon on March 18, including 6 in central Beirut apartment strikes. Israel issued its widest forced evacuation order in southern Lebanon since the 2006 war, ordering civilians south of the Zahrani River to move north. Cumulative Lebanon toll: **968 killed, 2,432 injured, 1 million+ displaced** since the war began.

### Houthis remain the critical wild card

Despite 20 days of war, **Houthis have NOT launched direct military operations**. Analysts cite strategic patience, a possible deal with the US and Saudis, and self-preservation after Israeli decapitation strikes in 2025 that killed senior Houthi leadership. Iran has threatened the Red Sea as "fair game" due to the USS Ford's presence. Bab el-Mandab traffic has been "sharply reduced" per Windward. The Houthis remain the conflict's "most unpredictable actor" — their potential entry would simultaneously threaten Red Sea shipping and the only viable alternative oil export route via Yanbu.

### Updated shipping attacks and Gulf state casualties

Two new vessel incidents were reported via UKMTO on March 18-19, bringing the total to approximately **22-23 attacks on commercial vessels** since the war began. Cumulative Gulf state deaths stand at **at least 21** across UAE (8), Saudi Arabia (2), Bahrain (2), Kuwait (1), Oman (2), and others. Iraqi deaths from all strikes total at least **61** (mostly PMF members).

## 3. A developing carrier gap puts naval operations under strain

### USS Gerald R. Ford — arriving Souda Bay for repairs

Ford departed the Red Sea for **NSA Souda Bay, Crete** for an estimated 1+ week of pierside repairs following a March 12 fire in the aft laundry facility. The fire destroyed **100+ berthing racks** and damaged space for 600+ sailors; over 200 were treated for smoke inhalation. The Navy pulled **1,000 mattresses from the under-construction USS John F. Kennedy (CVN-79)** at Newport News and shipped them to Ford. Ford's deployment has reached **268+ days** since June 24, 2025 — approaching post-Vietnam records. Ford's three destroyer escorts (Bainbridge, Mahan, Churchill) remain on station. Greek media reported investigators are exploring whether the fire was **deliberately set by crew frustrated with the extended deployment**. Some sources indicate Ford may transit home to Norfolk rather than return to station.

### USS George H.W. Bush — deployment imminent but unconfirmed

Bush completed COMPTUEX on March 5, certifying CSG-10 for deployment. Multiple outlets (19FortyFive, Maritime Executive) reported Bush was transiting toward the Mediterranean to relieve Ford as of March 11. USS Gonzalez (DDG-66) departed Norfolk March 16 for a scheduled deployment. **No official DoD confirmation of deployment orders** as of March 19, but all indicators point to transit underway. Carrier Air Wing 7 is embarked with approximately 90 aircraft.

### USS Tripoli ARG — one week from theater

The full three-ship Tripoli Amphibious Ready Group (Tripoli, San Diego, New Orleans) plus escorts (cruiser Robert Smalls, destroyer Rafael Peralta) **transited the Malacca Strait on March 17-18** carrying the 31st MEU (~2,200 Marines) with F-35B Lightning IIs. Estimated arrival in the Arabian Sea: **7-10 days** from the Singapore transit, putting it in theater around March 24-27.

### USS Abraham Lincoln — sole carrier on station

Lincoln is **the only carrier in the active combat zone** with Ford departing. Iranian Army propaganda claimed Lincoln was "forced to retreat" — debunked by CENTCOM. A Chinese MizarVision satellite intelligence report claimed Lincoln had pulled back to waters off southwestern Oman, but US commercial imagery has imposed a multi-day delay making verification impossible. Lincoln's CSG-3 includes destroyers Spruance, Michael Murphy, McFaul, Milius, Delbert D. Black, Pinckney, and Mitscher.

### Mine countermeasure capacity and allied naval developments

**LCS MCM ships** USS Tulsa and Santa Barbara were photographed at Penang, Malaysia on March 15 for a "logistics stop." USS Canberra is in the Indian Ocean. The **Pentagon now downplays the mine threat** relative to the missile threat — a significant shift from earlier reporting. No USS Florida SSGN operations are publicly confirmed; the Navy is extending Florida's service life rather than retiring it. **Tomahawk inventory strain** is critical: approximately **400 Tomahawks were expended in the first 96 hours** versus only 322 procured over the past 5 years.

**No allied Hormuz coalition is forming.** The EU, UK, Germany, France, Italy, Greece, Spain, Luxembourg, and Romania all explicitly declined military involvement. EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas stated "nobody wants to go actively in this war." However, **India deployed over half a dozen warships** to the Gulf of Oman/Arabian Sea for bilateral escort operations (will NOT enter the strait). Pakistan's Operation Muhafiz-ul-Bahr continues. **China deployed the Liaowang-1 signals intelligence vessel** (30,000 tons) to the Gulf of Oman, escorted by naval combatants — a new intelligence-gathering dimension.

## 4. Energy markets entered crisis territory as South Pars fallout spread

### Oil prices surged past $118 on dual energy escalation

- **Brent crude**: Spiked to **$118-$119/barrel** intraday on March 19, up from $107.38 the previous close — a gain of more than **10%** in a single session. The session range was $109.21-$119.11. Brent's 52-week high reached **$119.50**.
- **WTI crude**: Approached **$100/barrel**.
- **Diesel benchmark**: Trading "north of **$180/barrel**," the highest in nearly four years.
- The catalyst was the one-two punch of Israel's South Pars strike (cutting 70% of Iran's domestic gas supply) followed by Iran's retaliatory attacks on Ras Laffan, Yanbu SAMREF, Kuwaiti refineries, and UAE gas facilities.

### Stock markets hit 2026 lows

- **Dow Jones** (March 18 close): Lost **768 points (−1.63%)** to 46,225 — new 2026 low, closed below 200-day moving average.
- **S&P 500**: Fell **1.36%** to 6,624.70 — **lowest closing level of 2026**.
- **Nasdaq**: Shed **1.46%** to 22,152. **VIX** at **25.09**, up 12%.
- **March 19 futures**: S&P down 0.42%, Nasdaq down 0.56%.
- **Global markets March 19**: Nikkei **−3.38%**, Hang Seng −2.02%, FTSE −0.94%, DAX −0.96%, Euro Stoxx 50 −1.71%.
- Energy stocks outperformed: Occidental Petroleum up 36% Q1; ExxonMobil and Chevron at all-time highs.

### Gas prices, TTF, and consumer impact

- **US gas**: National average **$3.84-$3.88/gallon** (AAA/CNN), up from $2.98 pre-war — a **29% increase** in 19 days. Diesel near $5.07/gallon. Eight states above $4, three above $5. EIA revised its 2026 average forecast from $2.91 to **$3.34/gallon**.
- **European gas (TTF)**: Surged **23-35% to near €67/MWh** on March 19 — highest since early 2023. Pre-war TTF was €32-36/MWh. Goldman Sachs warned a monthlong Hormuz halt could push TTF to €74/MWh.
- **Trump waived the Jones Act** on March 18 (60-day temporary waiver) to ease fuel distribution constraints.

### Federal Reserve held steady amid uncertainty

The Fed held rates at **3.50-3.75%** with one dissent (Stephen Miran favoring a cut). Inflation forecast revised **up to 2.7%** from 2.5%. Powell: "Higher energy prices will push up overall inflation, but it is too soon to know the scope and duration." Seven of 19 FOMC participants projected NO rate cuts in 2026. Market rate cut expectations pushed out to **April 2027**. PPI rose 0.7% in February, year-over-year 3.4% — worse than expected.

### Strait of Hormuz and shipping

- **Traffic**: 8 non-Iranian vessels detected on March 17 (Windward) — "nearly double" recent days. Only **21 tankers total** have transited since the war began versus 100+/day pre-war. Iran allowing "permission-based transits to friendly countries" (Chinese, Indian, Pakistani-flagged mostly).
- **Stranded vessels**: Approximately **3,200 vessels** stranded west of the strait; ~**20,000-40,000 seafarers** impacted.
- **Insurance**: Hull war risk cost surged to **~5% of vessel value** — a $100M tanker pays $5M per transit. The strait is physically passable for some but "uninsurable" for most Western operators.
- **IEA SPR release**: 400M barrel drawdown (largest in IEA's 50-year history) began week of March 16 at ~1.4M bbl/day from US reserves. Market skepticism persists — crude prices surged 17%+ since announcement. Release covers only ~15% of supply lost from Hormuz closure.
- **IMO 36th Extraordinary Session** (March 18-19 in London): Proposed "safe maritime corridor" for evacuating stranded ships. Council vote on resolutions expected March 19 but are non-binding.
- **Bitcoin**: Trading **$73,500-$75,000**, up ~14% since the war began, outperforming gold, S&P 500, and Nasdaq. Spot Bitcoin ETF inflows exceeded $1.3B in March. Central Bank of Iran acquired $507M in Tether to support the rial.

### Alternative supply routes under threat

Saudi Arabia's Yanbu exports via the **East-West Pipeline** (5M bbl/day capacity) are now the primary Gulf crude export outlet, but the SAMREF refinery there was hit by an Iranian drone on March 19. The UAE's **ADCOP pipeline** to Fujairah provides additional capacity. Combined pipeline capacity of **3.5-5.5M bbl/day** cannot replace the 20M bbl/day that normally flows through Hormuz. Omani alternative ports (Duqm, Salalah, Sohar) have been degraded by Iranian drone strikes. The Red Sea route remains vulnerable to potential Houthi resumption.

## 5. Diplomatic landscape hardened as ceasefire prospects dimmed

### Iran explicitly rejects ceasefire

Iranian FM Abbas Araghchi told Al Jazeera on March 18: "We don't believe in a ceasefire. We believe in ending the war on all fronts." He rejected any suggestion Iran was seeking negotiations and said the US is "responsible for all the consequences of this war." This stance, combined with the killing of Larijani — a pragmatic figure who led nuclear negotiations — effectively **closes the most visible diplomatic channel** for near-term de-escalation.

### Riyadh emergency meeting reshapes regional alignment

Saudi Arabia hosted an **emergency consultative meeting of 12 Arab and Islamic foreign ministers** (March 18-19) from Azerbaijan, Bahrain, Egypt, Jordan, Kuwait, Lebanon, Pakistan, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Syria, Turkey, and UAE. Iranian ballistic missiles were intercepted over Riyadh **during the meeting itself**. The joint statement called on Iran to "immediately halt its attacks" and condemned "Iran's deliberate attacks using ballistic missiles and unmanned aerial vehicles that targeted residential areas and civilian infrastructure." Saudi FM Prince Faisal bin Farhan stated "almost nothing can re-establish trust" if Iran doesn't stop. This language represents a **qualitative shift** in Saudi Arabia's posture from cautious hedging to active confrontation readiness.

### Joe Kent resignation and domestic political fractures

**Joe Kent**, Director of the National Counterterrorism Center, resigned March 17 — the **highest-ranking Trump official to resign over the war**. A retired Green Beret with 11 combat tours and Gold Star spouse, Kent posted: "Iran posed no imminent threat to our nation, and it is clear that we started this war due to pressure from Israel and its powerful American lobby." He told Tucker Carlson there was "no intelligence" of an imminent Iranian attack. NBC News reported Kent is **under FBI investigation for allegedly leaking classified information**. Trump dismissed him: "I always thought he was weak on security." DNI Tulsi Gabbard declined to answer whether Iran's nuclear program posed an "imminent threat" at a March 18 Senate hearing, though she said the regime "appears to be intact" but "largely degraded."

### Congressional and budget developments

No new AUMF votes occurred March 18-19; War Powers Resolutions were previously defeated (Senate 47-53 on March 4, House 219-212 on March 5). The Pentagon is requesting a **$200+ billion supplemental** funding package. Hegseth confirmed: "It takes money to kill bad guys."

### European allies remain firmly on the sidelines

EU foreign ministers confirmed "no appetite" for extending Operation Aspides to Hormuz. German Chancellor Merz said deployment requires "an international mandate, which we do not currently have." French President Macron would consider escort operations "only after the war ends." UK PM Starmer said Britain "will not be drawn into the wider war" but is discussing use of mine-hunting drones already in the region. Luxembourg FM Bettel described Trump's approach as "blackmail." Trump responded: "I think NATO is making a very foolish mistake" and said the US should rethink NATO membership.

### IAEA and nuclear status

IAEA Chief Grossi, in Washington on March 18, stated the war **cannot entirely eliminate Iran's nuclear program**: "The material will still be there and the enrichment capacities will be there." The status of a new underground enrichment facility in Isfahan is unknown — inspectors were evacuated during June 2025 strikes and have not returned. DNI Gabbard told senators Iran's enrichment program was "obliterated" in prior strikes — a characterization at odds with Grossi's assessment.

### Humanitarian catastrophe deepening

- **Displacement**: UN estimates **600,000-1 million Iranian households (up to 3.2 million people)** displaced internally.
- **Internet**: Connectivity at **4% of normal levels** (NetBlocks). Iranians abroad unable to contact family.
- **Healthcare**: 25+ hospitals damaged, at least 9 forced out of service; 11 healthcare workers killed, 55 wounded.
- **Arrests**: Over 500 people arrested for sending information to media. Police chief warned anyone appearing on streets "will be treated as enemies."
- **Funeral**: Thousands attended a March 18 procession in Tehran's Enghelab Square for Larijani, Soleimani, and 84 Iranian Navy personnel killed aboard the destroyer Dena.
- **Trump attended the dignified transfer** at Dover AFB on March 18 for the 6 KC-135 crew killed in Iraq on March 12.
- **Ukraine deployed 200+ anti-drone military experts** to several Middle East countries to help defend against Iranian-designed drones.

## Conclusion: a war that keeps expanding rather than concluding

Day 20 of Operation Epic Fury revealed three compounding dynamics that challenge any near-term off-ramp. First, the **South Pars escalation cycle** created a new axis of conflict between Iran and its Gulf neighbors, transforming what were reluctant bystanders into potential combatants — Saudi Arabia's explicit warning of military action and Qatar's invocation of Article 51 represent genuine inflection points. Second, the **carrier gap** created by Ford's departure to Crete leaves Lincoln as the sole carrier in a combat zone that now spans from the Caspian Sea to the Strait of Hormuz to Lebanon, with Bush's deployment still officially unconfirmed and Tripoli a week away. Third, the **dual economic shocks** — energy infrastructure attacks across four Gulf states and the Fed's hawkish hold amid surging inflation — are transmitting conflict costs to global consumers at an accelerating rate. Brent's approach toward $120 and TTF's 35% single-day surge represent the kind of price signals that historically trigger demand destruction and recession.

The Houthi wildcard remains the single most consequential unknown. Their entry would simultaneously threaten Red Sea shipping and the Yanbu export route that is currently the Gulf's lifeline. With **~3,200 vessels stranded**, insurance costs at 5% of hull value, and the IEA's historic stockpile release covering only 15% of lost Hormuz flows, the economic architecture sustaining global energy markets is under unprecedented strain. The killing of Larijani — the regime's most prominent pragmatist — combined with Araghchi's explicit rejection of ceasefire talks, leaves no visible diplomatic pathway. The Pentagon's $200 billion supplemental request and Hegseth's promise of ever-larger strike packages signal Washington is planning for a protracted campaign, even as the war's costs mount daily across every metric that matters.
