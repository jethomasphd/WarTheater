# Daily financial tracker: Operation Epic Fury's first 16 days

**The US-Iran conflict that began February 28, 2026 triggered the most severe oil supply disruption in history, sending Brent crude from $71 to $103 per barrel, gasoline up 70 cents per gallon, and the S&P 500 to its 2026 low — all while accumulating an estimated $16.5–20 billion in direct military costs in just over two weeks.** This report compiles day-by-day data across six financial and military spending metrics from February 27 through March 15, 2026, drawing on CNBC, EIA, FRED, AAA, CSIS, and Pentagon briefings to Congress.

## Oil prices surged 45% in two weeks on the largest supply disruption ever

Brent crude opened the period at **$71.32** on February 27 — a normal, even subdued price — and closed at **$103.14** on March 13, a gain of nearly 45%. WTI followed a similar arc, rising from roughly **$66.50 to $98.71**. The catalyst was Iran's effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which disrupted approximately **20 million barrels per day** of global oil transit, the largest supply disruption in recorded history.

The price action was extraordinarily volatile. On March 9, Brent touched an intraday high of approximately **$119.50** before settling at $98.96 — a single-day trading range exceeding $39 per barrel. The following day, March 10, a false report from Energy Secretary Chris Wright about a Navy escort through the Strait sent Brent crashing **11.3%** to $87.80, with WTI plunging as low as $77 intraday before settling at $83.45. By March 12, Brent crossed **$100 for the first time since August 2022** after Iran's new Supreme Leader vowed the Strait would "remain shut."

OPEC+ announced a 206,000 bpd output increase effective April on March 1, and the IEA coordinated a record **400-million-barrel emergency reserve release** on March 11 (including 172 million barrels from the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve). Neither intervention materially dampened prices given the scale of the disruption. Oil price sources include CNBC settlement reports, FRED/EIA spot prices, CNN, and SunSirs commodity data. Prices for March 4 and some other dates are interpolated from surrounding confirmed data points.

## Gasoline jumped 70 cents as Americans felt the war at the pump

The national average price of regular unleaded gasoline rose from approximately **$2.99 per gallon** on February 27 to **$3.675** on March 14 — an increase of roughly **$0.69 (23%)** in just over two weeks. GasBuddy called the week of March 2–9 the "**largest weekly gas price jump of the century**," with prices rising 49 cents in seven days. California stations exceeded $5.00 per gallon.

EIA weekly data captures the acceleration: **$2.937** for the week ending February 23, **$3.015** for the week ending March 2, and **$3.502** for the week ending March 9 — a single-week leap of nearly 49 cents, the largest since the Russia-Ukraine shock of March 2022. AAA's daily data, sourced from OPIS credit card transactions at roughly 130,000 stations, provides finer granularity. Confirmed AAA data points include $3.11 (Mar 3), $3.251 (Mar 5), $3.413 (Mar 7), $3.598 (Mar 12), $3.630 (Mar 13), and $3.675 (Mar 14). Dates marked with "~" in the table are interpolations between these confirmed readings.

The EIA's Short-Term Energy Outlook, published March 10, forecast a March 2026 monthly average of **$3.58** — a figure that the daily data suggests will be exceeded as prices continued to climb through mid-March.

## The S&P 500 fell 3.6% to its 2026 low as war risk repriced equities

The S&P 500 declined from **6,878.88** on February 27 to **6,632.19** on March 13, a drop of **246 points (3.6%)** and a new closing low for 2026. The index posted three consecutive weekly losses. All closing values are confirmed via CNBC daily market reports and cross-verified with FRED and Yahoo Finance.

Markets showed surprising resilience on the first post-strike trading day (March 2), recovering from a 1.2% intraday decline to close essentially flat at 6,881.62. The sharpest single-day losses came on **March 6 (−1.33%)** and **March 12 (−1.52%)**, the latter driven by Brent breaching $100. The index briefly rallied on March 9 after President Trump suggested the war was "very complete, pretty much," but gains evaporated over the following sessions. By March 13, the S&P sat roughly **5% below its January 27, 2026 all-time high**.

## War costs reached an estimated $16.5–20 billion in 16 days

No single authoritative daily cost figure exists, but several high-credibility anchor points allow reasonable estimation. The Pentagon briefed the Senate Appropriations Defense Subcommittee that the **first six days of operations cost $11.3 billion**, a figure reported by the New York Times, NBC News, Military Times, and Fox News, and described as "roughly accurate" by Senator Chris Coons. CSIS analysts Mark Cancian and Chris Park published an updated estimate on March 13 pegging costs through **Day 12 at approximately $16.5 billion**.

The daily cost trajectory reflects a front-loaded spending pattern typical of air campaigns. The Washington Post reported **$5.6 billion in munitions expenditure in the first 48 hours alone**, consistent with massive initial strikes that hit over 6,000 targets. Penn Wharton's Kent Smetters estimated daily costs of roughly **$2 billion per day** during the opening phase, declining to approximately **$800 million per day** as operations shifted from initial strikes to sustained operations. The daily estimates in the table above are constructed by fitting a declining curve through the confirmed anchor points: $5.6B cumulative at Day 2, $11.3B at Day 6, and $16.5B at Day 12.

Key cost drivers identified by CSIS include:

- **Munitions replacement** (~$3.1B in the first 100 hours alone), with production rates unable to fully replenish stocks consumed
- **Interceptor costs** ($1.2B–$3.7B in the first 100 hours), with THAAD deliveries paused since August 2023
- **Combat losses and base damage** (~$1.7B through Day 12)
- **Pre-war buildup costs** (~$630M, per former Pentagon Comptroller Elaine McCusker at AEI)

The Pentagon is preparing a **~$50 billion supplemental funding request**, led by Deputy Defense Secretary Steve Feinberg. Penn Wharton projects total direct budgetary costs of **$40–95 billion** (central estimate ~$65B) if the conflict lasts two months, with additional US economic losses of **$50–210 billion** from energy disruption and trade impacts. The CBO has not yet published a dedicated cost analysis, though Representative Brendan Boyle has formally requested one.

## Methodology and source confidence

**High-confidence data** (confirmed by primary sources): S&P 500 closing values for all 11 trading days (CNBC, FRED, Yahoo Finance); Brent and WTI settlement prices for March 3, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13 (CNBC, CNN, SunSirs); AAA gas prices for March 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 12, 13, 14; Pentagon cumulative war cost of $11.3B through Day 6; CSIS cumulative estimate of $16.5B through Day 12.

**Medium-confidence data** (derived or back-calculated): Brent and WTI for February 27 and March 2 (FRED spot prices and back-calculation from CNBC percentage changes); oil prices for March 5 and 6 (derived from CNN/Barchart daily change data); gas prices for dates between confirmed AAA readings.

**Low-confidence data** (interpolated estimates): Oil prices for March 4 (no confirmed settlement found from any source); all daily war cost figures between anchor points; gas price for March 15 (not yet reported).

**War cost methodology**: Daily estimates were constructed by fitting a declining spending curve — consistent with Penn Wharton's characterization of ~$2B/day initial costs declining to ~$800M/day — through three anchor points: the Washington Post's $5.6B munitions figure at 48 hours, the Pentagon's $11.3B at Day 6, and CSIS's $16.5B at Day 12. The $50B supplemental request and Penn Wharton's $40–95B total projection suggest ongoing daily costs of $0.8–1.0B will persist for weeks.

## Conclusion

The 17 calendar days from February 27 through March 15, 2026 represent one of the most dramatic periods in commodity market history. Three numbers capture the scale: Brent crude rose **45%**, the S&P 500 fell **3.6%** to its 2026 low, and estimated direct military costs reached roughly **$20 billion** — all in just over two weeks. The disconnect between the relatively modest equity decline and the enormous commodity shock reflects markets pricing this as a temporary supply disruption rather than a structural economic crisis, though that assumption will be tested if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed. The daily cost run rate of approximately $850 million is tracking close to the early months of the 2003 Iraq invasion in inflation-adjusted terms, but the economic damage from energy price disruption — Penn Wharton's $50–210 billion estimate — dwarfs the direct military spending.
