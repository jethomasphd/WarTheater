# Iranian retaliation strikes since Operation Epic Fury began

**Iran's retaliatory campaign — launched within hours of the joint U.S.-Israeli assault on February 28, 2026 — has encompassed more than 500 ballistic missiles, ~2,000 drones, maritime attacks on 19+ commercial vessels, naval mine deployments in the Strait of Hormuz, and proxy offensives by Hezbollah and Iraqi militias across at least 10 countries.** As of March 15, 2026 (Day 16), these strikes have killed at least 15 people in Israel, 13 U.S. service members, 30+ civilians across Gulf states, and 8+ seafarers — while forcing the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz and triggering the worst oil-supply disruption in decades. Iran designated its retaliation "Operation True Promise IV," striking Israel, Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Oman, Jordan, Iraq, and Cyprus. What follows is the most exhaustive chronological reconstruction available from open sources.

## How Iran's retaliation evolved over 16 days

Iran's campaign followed a clear arc. The first 48 hours saw the highest volume — ~170 missiles at Israel on Day 1 alone, plus simultaneous saturation attacks across every Gulf state. By Day 5, ballistic missile fire had fallen **86%**, and by Day 7, it was down **90%**. Analysts attributed this to a combination of Israeli destruction of three-quarters of Iranian launch infrastructure, deliberate rationing of remaining stockpiles, and a strategic pivot toward asymmetric tactics.

From Day 9 onward, Iran shifted to **cluster munition warheads** that complicated Israeli air defenses — even intercepted missiles could release submunitions before destruction. Simultaneously, Iran escalated maritime warfare: mining the Strait of Hormuz, attacking commercial vessels with explosive boats and drones, and effectively shutting down **97% of Strait traffic**. The March 11 "maritime massacre" — five vessels hit in a single day — marked the most intense period of shipping attacks.

Iran also shifted toward **economic warfare**, targeting oil infrastructure (Ras Tanura, Bapco Sitra, Ruwais, Fujairah, QatarEnergy LNG), airports (Dubai International, Kuwait, Abu Dhabi, Bahrain), and even data centers. By mid-March, oil had surged past **$100/barrel**, the Strait was functionally closed, and the IEA had authorized release of **400 million barrels** from strategic reserves — the largest emergency drawdown in history.

The proxy dimension proved uneven. **Hezbollah** was the most active, launching 210+ missiles into Israel by Day 8 and peaking with a massive barrage on March 11. **Iraqi militias** claimed 291 operations by Day 12, mostly drone harassment of U.S. bases in Erbil and Baghdad. The **Houthis**, notably, did not fire a single confirmed shot — internal debate, depleted arsenals, and fear of Israeli retaliation kept them on the sidelines despite rhetorical solidarity and warnings that their "fingers are on the trigger."

By March 15, Iran's offensive capability was estimated to be **80% degraded**. Yet its capacity for asymmetric disruption — mines, drones, explosive boats, proxy rockets — remained sufficient to impose significant costs. The Strait of Hormuz remained effectively closed, and Iran continued launching reduced-rate missile salvos at Israel averaging ~10 attack waves per day, even as most were intercepted or fell in open areas. The war was far from over, but the balance of destruction was overwhelmingly asymmetric — with Iran absorbing vastly greater damage than it was able to inflict.
