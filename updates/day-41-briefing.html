01 — Ceasefire Status
Holding in Iran. Collapsing Over Lebanon.

The two-week ceasefire on direct US-Iran hostilities has technically held for approximately 45 hours as of this briefing. No confirmed US or Israeli strikes on Iranian territory since 8 PM ET April 7. CENTCOM has not issued any post-ceasefire strike statements. The air campaign appears genuinely paused inside Iranian airspace.

But the ceasefire's periphery is collapsing. Netanyahu stated at an April 8 press conference: "The two-week ceasefire does not include Lebanon. We still have some goals to accomplish." The White House confirmed this position. But Pakistan's PM Sharif — the mediator who brokered the deal — said it covered "everywhere, including Lebanon." Iran's position is unambiguous: Lebanon is included. European leaders from nine countries and the EU issued a joint statement demanding Lebanon's inclusion.

Iran's response was to re-close the Strait of Hormuz on April 8, accusing Israel of violating the ceasefire through its Lebanon operations. Iran's parliamentary speaker formally accused the US of breaching the agreement. Hezbollah launched retaliatory missiles against northern Israel in the early hours of April 9.

⚠ SOURCE CONFLICT: The exact status of Hormuz transit varies by source. White House (Leavitt) states "the Strait is open." Bloomberg, CNBC, ABC News, and Windward Maritime Intelligence all report it is functionally re-closed after the April 8 Lebanon strikes. War-risk insurance remains unavailable for mainstream commercial operators.

03 — Diplomacy
Islamabad Talks Loom Under a Cracking Ceasefire

The negotiations scheduled for April 10–11 in Islamabad remain the most consequential diplomatic event of the conflict. VP Vance leads the US delegation, joined by Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner — the highest-level US-Iran face-to-face since the 1979 hostage crisis. Pakistan's PM Sharif and Field Marshal Munir serve as mediators, having maintained the sole communication channel between Washington and Tehran throughout the crisis.

The negotiating framework is built on Iran's 10-point counter-proposal, which Trump called "a workable basis." Iran's core demands: no enrichment concessions without sanctions relief, controlled Hormuz passage coordinated with Iran's armed forces, permanent ceasefire inclusive of Lebanon, and release of frozen assets. The US demands: unconditional Hormuz reopening "without limitation, including tolls," verifiable dismantlement of nuclear weapons-grade enrichment, and cessation of proxy warfare.

The gap remains enormous. But the ceasefire's collapse over Lebanon has introduced a new dimension: Iran may refuse to attend unless the Lebanon question is resolved. Conversely, Trump's April 9 "next conquest" rhetoric suggests Washington sees the military balance as leverage, not compromise.

⚠ TIMING DISCREPANCY: Pakistan's initial invitation specified Friday April 10; the White House confirmed "Saturday morning local time" (April 11 Islamabad time). The operational timeline spans both dates. Iran's delegation composition has not been publicly confirmed.
04 — Force Posture
No Drawdown. Bush CSG Arriving This Week.

The US military has not stood down a single asset since the ceasefire. Approximately 50,000+ troops remain deployed across the region with 108 ships (78 USS, 30 USNS). Secretary of War Hegseth and CJCS Gen. Caine stated on April 8 that forces will remain in theater.

USS Abraham Lincoln (CVN-72) continues as the primary striking force in the Arabian Sea, ~150 miles off Oman's coast. CSG-3 with CVW-9 conducted flight operations as recently as April 7.

USS George H.W. Bush (CVN-77) — CSG-10, commanded by RADM Alexis Walker — is transiting the Atlantic with ETA April 10–12. Arrival coincides with the Islamabad talks and gives CENTCOM dual-carrier capability for the first time since Ford was sidelined.

The USS Boxer (LHD-4) with the 11th MEU departed Pearl Harbor April 1 and was operating in the western Pacific as of April 8. The 82nd Airborne's 1st BCT (~2,000 paratroopers) remains forward-deployed in the Middle East. The French carrier Charles de Gaulle remains in the eastern Mediterranean near Crete with an expanded multinational escort.

CENTCOM's final pre-ceasefire assessment: 13,000+ targets struck, ~80% of Iran's air defenses destroyed, 150 warships sunk/damaged across 16 classes, 90% of weapons factories destroyed, and Iran's air force reduced to zero operational flights per day.

05 — Energy & Hormuz
The Strait Remains the Chokepoint

Despite the ceasefire's explicit requirement for "complete, immediate, and safe opening" of the Strait, Hormuz remains functionally blockaded. Iran offered only IRGC-controlled, permission-based transit with proposed tolls — far short of the unconditional access Washington demands.

On April 7, 11 vessels transited — all under IRGC supervision, mostly sanctioned Iranian-trading tankers. On April 8, only 5 bulk carriers passed before Iran re-closed the strait citing the Lebanon strikes. As of April 9, status is uncertain. Approximately 800 tankers and 3,200 total vessels with ~20,000 seafarers remain trapped inside the Persian Gulf.

Windward Maritime Intelligence's assessment: "The strait has not reopened — it is in a supervised pause." War-risk insurance remains unavailable for mainstream operators, effectively blocking transit regardless of Iran's stated position.

Gas nationally hit $4.166/gallon (AAA, April 9) — up $1.19 from pre-war $2.98. California at ~$5.93; Oklahoma lowest at ~$3.48. OPEC crude output collapsed by 7.56 million b/d (~25%) in March — the biggest monthly drop in four decades. The IEA coordinated its largest-ever emergency release: 400 million barrels from 32 nations. The US authorized 172 million barrels from the SPR.

Casualty figures remain deeply contested. Iran's internet connectivity stands at 4% of normal, making independent verification nearly impossible. The threefold divergence between the Health Ministry (2,076), HRANA (3,636), and Hengaw (7,300+) reflects Iran's systematic underreporting of military deaths, Hengaw's inclusion of estimated military casualties the government does not acknowledge, and the fog created by 40 days of sustained bombardment across 195 cities in 27 provinces.

The IAEA confirmed four strikes near the Bushehr nuclear power plant, the closest hitting just 75 meters from the reactor perimeter. One plant employee was killed. The reactor itself remains undamaged with no radiation release. Rosatom evacuated 198 Russian staff.

In Lebanon, the April 8 strikes killed 254 people — the single deadliest day of the entire war on any front. Over 1 million Lebanese have been displaced, with 700,000+ in shelters. WHO has verified 23 attacks on healthcare in Iran causing 9 deaths.

07 — Domestic Politics
No Rally. 61% Disapprove.

The war has produced no "rally around the flag" effect. Trump's overall approval stands at 35–41% depending on the poll (CNN/SSRS: 35%, YouGov: 37%, RCP average: ~41%). War-specific disapproval is higher: Pew Research found 61% disapprove of Trump's handling, 59% say force was wrong, and only 22% believe military action has made the US safer.

The $200B supplemental has not been formally submitted to Congress. Opposition spans both parties. The ceasefire may reduce immediate urgency but the funding question — along with war powers and accountability debates — remains unresolved. Trump is underwater in 135 Republican-held congressional seats, creating significant midterm exposure.

08 — Regional Actors
Houthis Poised, Hezbollah Retaliating, Gulf States Battered

Houthis have resumed strikes on Israel since March 28 and threaten to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait. They have not attacked commercial shipping since the ceasefire but Iran is reportedly pushing them to prepare for a renewed Red Sea campaign. Their ceasefire posture is unclear.

Hezbollah initially halted attacks when the ceasefire was announced. After "Operation Eternal Darkness," they launched retaliatory missiles against northern Israel on April 9. The IDF admitted it overestimated damage to Hezbollah from the 2024 war — the group has rebuilt with hundreds of launchers and tens of thousands of rockets.

Iraq PMF fighters have entered Iran through the Shalamcheh border crossing, deploying to Khuzestan province. Baghdad formally authorized PMF self-defense. PMF-allied groups struck Baghdad International Airport's diplomatic support center during the ceasefire.

Gulf states welcomed the ceasefire but demand enforceable guarantees. UAE intercepted 35 drone attacks; Kuwait intercepted 28; Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline was struck. All reject any arrangement leaving Hormuz under Iranian toll control. The consensus: pause is welcome, but the threat of Iranian aggression must be structurally eliminated — not just paused.

10 — Analyst Note
The Architecture of an Unstable Pause

This ceasefire was never designed to hold. It was brokered in 88 minutes under threat of civilizational destruction, between parties that disagree on its fundamental scope. The Lebanon exclusion is not a diplomatic wrinkle — it is a structural contradiction that makes the agreement internally incoherent. Iran cannot accept a deal that leaves its most important regional ally exposed to systematic destruction; Israel will not accept constraints on its northern front; and the United States has chosen to treat these as separable conflicts when they are, for every other party, a single war.

The next 72 hours will determine whether Islamabad produces a framework that addresses this contradiction or whether the ceasefire becomes another data point in the conflict's escalation curve. The military infrastructure for resumption is fully intact on all sides. The USS George H.W. Bush arrives in theater this week. The 82nd Airborne is forward-deployed. Iran's missile stocks are depleted but not exhausted. And oil, the real-time ceasefire confidence indicator, is already climbing back.
