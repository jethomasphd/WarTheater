The war is paused, not over.

The Ceasefire Deal

The dramatic reversal from civilizational threat to ceasefire in roughly 10 hours represents one of the most extraordinary pivots in modern wartime diplomacy. That morning, Trump had posted “A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again. I don’t want that to happen, but it probably will.” Iranians had organized human chains around power plants as shields. By evening, the same president who threatened to erase a civilization was extending an olive branch framed as magnanimity.

The mechanics of the deal reveal Pakistan’s emerging role as the conflict’s indispensable mediator. Field Marshal Munir maintained simultaneous contact with Vance, Witkoff, and Araghchi throughout the night. Witkoff and Araghchi exchanged direct text messages during the final push — the closest thing to direct U.S.-Iran communication since the conflict began. Trump’s Truth Social post described the deal as conditional on “the COMPLETE, IMMEDIATE, and SAFE OPENING of the Strait of Hormuz.” Iran’s acceptance language was deliberately narrower: Araghchi offered “coordinated safe passage” with “due consideration of technical limitations” — Iran retains a gatekeeper role. Whether that gap collapses the talks before they begin is the question of the next 48 hours.

The Negotiating Chasm

The published positions of the two sides remain diametrically opposed on the core issue. Iran’s 10-point proposal demands a U.S. non-aggression guarantee, recognition of uranium enrichment rights, an end to the war against all Axis of Resistance components including Hezbollah and Houthis, withdrawal of U.S. combat forces from all regional bases, lifting of all sanctions, and full war reparations. Notably absent: any commitment to limit Iran’s nuclear program. The U.S. 15-point proposal demands dismantling of nuclear facilities at Natanz, Isfahan, and Fordow; permanent commitment to never develop nuclear weapons; handover of enriched uranium to the IAEA; and unconditional Strait of Hormuz reopening.

Trump’s assertion that “most of them have been fully negotiated” and that the public proposals differ from private talks suggests significant behind-the-scenes movement. But the IAEA has been unable to verify the status of Iran’s approximately 440 kg of 60% enriched uranium — enough for roughly 10 nuclear weapons if further enriched — since before the strikes began. This verification gap may prove the most intractable issue at Islamabad.

Before the ceasefire defused it, Trump’s “whole civilization will die tonight” post triggered the most serious bipartisan backlash of his presidency. The ceasefire buys Trump political breathing room but does not erase the damage. If the Islamabad talks fail and the war resumes, Trump will face it with depleted domestic support, an Iranian population that organized human shields, and a party that briefly questioned his fitness to command.
