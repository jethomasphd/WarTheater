Coercive Diplomacy Under a Blockade: CENTCOM Tallies, BBC Verify Dissents, “Locked and Loaded”

The blockade kept turning hulls around and still failed to match its rhetoric. CENTCOM’s 72-hour tally climbed on a clean curve — 6 vessels turned back in the first 24 hours, 9 by 48 hours, 13–14 by 72 hours. The single highest-value interdiction landed on Apr 14: the USS Spruance (DDG-111) turned back an Iranian-flagged cargo vessel in the Gulf of Oman (Stars & Stripes, Apr 16), a scalp that Adm. Brad Cooper can credibly cite when describing the blockade as “fully implemented.” CENTCOM reports more than 10,000 sailors, Marines, and airmen enforcing the line with “more than a dozen warships and dozens of aircraft.” But BBC Verify, working with MarineTraffic and gCaptain, explicitly disputes the “zero penetrations” claim: at least 4 Iran-related ships transited the Strait Apr 14. Hormuz transit volume sits below 10% of pre-war average.

The diplomatic envelope did not widen. Australia is not joining; Qatar rejected “any escalatory moves”; China’s MFA called the blockade “dangerous and irresponsible”; Russian FM Lavrov, in Beijing, declared “no military solution.” Treasury moved the financial escalation forward instead: Secretary Bessent described secondary sanctions as “the financial equivalent of what we saw in the kinetic activities,” designated 3 individuals, 17 entities, and 9 oil tankers tied to Mohammad Hossein Shamkhani, and halted 30-day waivers on Iranian and Russian oil. The rhetorical deterrence ladder is steepening even as the shooting remains paused.

Both sides are negotiating with one hand and preparing for a second act with the other.
