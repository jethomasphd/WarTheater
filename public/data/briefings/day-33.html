The Bnei Brak Attack: A New Escalatory Threshold

The IRGC’s use of cluster bomb warheads against a densely populated residential area in Bnei Brak represents a qualitative escalation in the conflict. The image of a 10-year-old girl critically injured by glass shrapnel while sleeping in her bed, and a 13-year-old boy seriously wounded while trying to wake his deaf mother — neither able to reach shelter — will dominate international media coverage and reshape the political dynamics of any ceasefire negotiations. Cluster munitions are banned under the 2008 Convention on Cluster Munitions (which neither the US, Israel, nor Iran have signed), and their use against civilian areas is widely condemned regardless of treaty status.

Iran’s three-wave attack within a single hour, combined with the IRGC’s claim of “100+ heavy missiles and attack drones” and “200+ rockets,” constitutes the most complex single-day attack package of the war. Yet the tactical picture suggests diminishing capability: the second and third volleys were largely intercepted, a fourth salvo was defeated entirely, and Hegseth confirmed that Iran’s drone attacks are down 95% from peak. The IRGC appears to be concentrating its remaining offensive capacity into dramatic but declining salvos — trading volume for psychological impact.
