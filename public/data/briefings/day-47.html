The Blockade, First Full Day: Three Ships, No Confrontation

April 14 was the first full day of the US naval blockade of Iranian ports, and the Strait of Hormuz came close to resembling a commercial graveyard. Only three vessels transited the Strait in the 24-hour window. None was bound for an Iranian port, so the blockade was never directly tested. The Rich Starry — Chinese-owned, Malawi-flagged, US-sanctioned, carrying 250,000 barrels of methanol — was initially turned back near Qeshm Island before passing overnight. The Murlikishan, also US-sanctioned and with a documented history of carrying Russian and Iranian oil, completed its run. The Panama-flagged Peace Gulf pushed through to the UAE via an IRGC-ordered route. Set against a pre-war baseline of roughly 140 transits per day, the aperture has collapsed to a trickle; the blockade itself has not yet had to do anything.

The IEA’s April Oil Market Report, released the same day, supplied the structural arithmetic behind the empty shipping lane. Global oil supply fell 10.1 mb/d in March to 97 mb/d — the largest disruption in the history of the global oil market. Hormuz throughput dropped to 3.8 mb/d, an 80%+ decline from the pre-war baseline above 20 mb/d. IEA Director Fatih Birol’s headline assessment: “Worse than 1973, 1979, and 2022 combined.” The IEA projects the steepest quarterly demand decline since COVID, with Q2 demand falling 1.5 mb/d y/y; its 2026 global oil demand forecast swung by 810,000 b/d — from +730K growth to a –80K decline. Cumulative March supply losses already exceed 360 million barrels, with another 440 million projected for April. Russia’s oil revenues nearly doubled to $19 billion in March as buyers pivoted away from Gulf crude. US Energy Secretary Chris Wright said prices will remain elevated until “meaningful ship traffic” returns to the Strait.

On the water, CENTCOM is still working the clearance problem rather than the interdiction problem. USS Pioneer and USS Chief have been dispatched from Singapore to reinforce the mine-clearance effort. CENTCOM commander Adm. Brad Cooper said forces are establishing “a new passage” through the Strait for commercial traffic and will share the routing with the maritime industry. Dark-vessel activity remained elevated — three non-AIS tankers observed berthing at Iranian ports, fourteen non-AIS vessels in waiting areas — suggesting Iranian-adjacent commerce is migrating off the tracking grid rather than halting entirely. Behind the scenes, France broke from Washington and announced preparations for a “peaceful multinational mission aimed at restoring freedom of navigation” in Hormuz, explicitly positioned as separate from US belligerent operations. China called the blockade “dangerous and irresponsible.” The UK’s prior refusal to join stands. The cost to Iran from the blockade alone is estimated at roughly $435 million per day (FDD) — and so far, Tehran has chosen to absorb it rather than test it.

Bint Jbeil, Qassem’s Refusal, and the Broken Half of the Truce

If the Iran front is a frozen lake, the Lebanon front is a fire still spreading. The Iran-front ceasefire has now held for approximately 168 hours with zero confirmed new US or Israeli strikes on Iranian territory and zero Iranian retaliatory salvos on April 13–14. But the Battle of Bint Jbeil is now in its fifth–sixth day, and the geography alone is historically loaded: the IDF’s 98th Division has advanced to within 300–400 meters of the Bint Jbeil stadium — the site where Hassan Nasrallah delivered his 2000 “spider’s web” speech after the Israeli withdrawal. An estimated 100+ Hezbollah fighters have been killed in the battle, with dozens still active. The IDF raided a hospital where armed Hezbollah operatives were sheltering. On April 13, two IDF soldiers were moderately wounded and six lightly wounded by a Hezbollah drone attack; a woman was injured in Nahariya by a Hezbollah rocket; an ICRC center was struck in Tyre, killing one person. A Lebanese Red Cross paramedic had been killed the day before by an Israeli drone that “directly targeted” the team (NBC News).

The cumulative Lebanese toll, per the Lebanon Public Health Emergency Operation Center on April 14: 2,089+ killed and 6,762+ wounded — up by 34 killed and 174 wounded from Day 45, and by 69 / 326 from the April 11 baseline of 2,020. The casualty accounting since the March 2 escalation now includes 252 women, 166 children, and 88 medical workers killed; UNICEF puts the total number of Lebanese children killed or injured at 600+. The April 8 “Black Wednesday” single-day toll is now documented at 357 killed after 50 jets dropped roughly 160 bombs on 100 targets in ten minutes. IDF forced-evacuation orders now cover approximately 14% of Lebanon’s territory (1,470 sq km). Displacement holds above one million — roughly 20% of the population — with 200,000+ fled to Syria. Nine bridges over the Litani have been destroyed; the lone lifeline, the Qasmieh Bridge, serves 71,000+ people in the Tyre area. WHO has documented 77 attacks on healthcare in Lebanon and 53 health-worker deaths.

The diplomatic thread was thin on Day 45 and became thinner on Day 46. Hezbollah Secretary-General Naim Qassem preemptively rejected Tuesday’s historic Lebanon-Israel talks at the US State Department, calling them “pointless” and “a free concession.” Ambassador Leiter restated that Israel “refused to discuss a ceasefire with the Hezbollah terrorist organisation”; PM Salam demands a ceasefire as precondition and has postponed his Washington trip; Iran and Pakistan continue to insist Lebanon was always inside the ceasefire framework (VP Vance: “we never made that promise”). CBS News reported that Trump had initially agreed to include Lebanon before reversing after a call with Netanyahu. The net result is that the most consequential diplomatic event of Day 47 — the first direct Israel-Lebanon meeting since 1993, with Secretary of State Rubio present — begins with the parties defining success in incompatible terms. One side wants Hezbollah disarmament before a ceasefire; the other refuses to enter the room without a ceasefire first.
