Project Freedom Collapsed Because the Saudis and Kuwaitis Quietly Killed It

The cleanest single explanation for the Project Freedom pause is not the CMA CGM San Antonio cruise-missile strike and not Pakistani-mediated MoU progress — both of which Trump cited — but the GCC withdrawal that NBC News (May 6) and AP (May 7) confirmed by Day 69. Two U.S. officials told NBC that Saudi Arabia suspended access to its territory and airspace for the Project Freedom mission; a Saudi official told AP, “We told them we are not part of this and that they can’t use our territories and bases for this”; Kuwait followed the same posture. The kingdom sent a parallel message to Tehran that it would not be involved — a deliberate signal of detachment to both warring parties. WSJ (via Times of Israel summary) reports the curbs ended once Project Freedom paused; the GCC pulled the floor and the operation collapsed onto it.

This is the most consequential GCC–U.S. divergence of the war. Even Sunni Gulf monarchies under direct Iranian fire — Fujairah Oil Industry Zone hit May 4, ADNOC’s Barakah tanker hit, UAE air defenses engaging missiles two consecutive days — would not back a U.S. force-projection mission they perceived as escalatory and uncoordinated. In the dual-blockade architecture, only two U.S.-flagged commercial vessels had transited under escort in the entire 48-hour Project Freedom window before the pause; the operation was signaling, not throughput. The Saudi message to Iran is the analytically important component: Riyadh is publicly declaring that it does not consider itself bound to the U.S. escalation ladder. That repositions every future U.S. kinetic option that would touch Saudi airspace, basing, or refueling.

The structural implication for the MoU track is dual-edged. On one hand, the GCC withdrawal validates the Rubio “concluded” framing — even if Trump wanted to resume offensive operations, the regional access has been quietly downgraded, and Trump’s “much higher level and intensity” rhetorical threat is now mostly a leverage device rather than a near-term capability statement. On the other hand, this hands Tehran a strategic gift: Iran can negotiate the MoU knowing the U.S. cannot easily reconstitute the Project Freedom posture without Saudi consent that has just been publicly withheld. Watch the next 72 hours for whether Riyadh restores access quietly once the MoU lands, or whether the curbs persist as a structural shift in GCC posture independent of the deal’s success.

Israel Broke the Lebanon Ceasefire in Beirut on the Eve of Iran’s MoU Response

The May 6 IDF strike on Beirut’s Dahiyeh — the first since the April 17 ceasefire — was timed deliberately. Hours before Iran’s informal Thursday MoU response deadline, the IDF struck Haret Hreik / Ghobeiri and killed Hezbollah Radwan Force Operations Commander Ahmed Ghaleb Ballout (rendered “Malek Ballout” in AFP’s source-close-to-Hezbollah reporting), Nasr regional intel chief Muhammad Ali Bazi, and Aerial Defense Chief Hussein Hassan. Per an Israeli source to CNN, the operation was coordinated in advance with the United States. The IDF disclosed it has killed 220+ Hezbollah operatives in past few weeks (85 in past week), and 11+ killed in concurrent strikes across south and east Lebanon May 6–7. UNHCR’s 380-killed-since-April-17 figure is on a tempo well above 10/day, and the Beirut strike marks the geographic re-expansion the dashboard has flagged for two weeks.

The operational reality on Day 69 is the opposite: Iran-track and Lebanon-track are now decoupled even where the diplomatic text links them. Israel’s preemptive Radwan strike timed for the eve of Iran’s response is a deliberate escalation signal — either to demonstrate that an MoU does not constrain Israel’s operational latitude in Lebanon, or to provoke a Hezbollah response that would let Israel argue the MoU’s Lebanon clause is unenforceable. Rubio’s 5 May aside that “a peace deal between Lebanon and Israel is imminently achievable… the problem is Hezbollah” reads on Day 69 as administrative permission for IDF kinetic latitude to continue under the MoU’s diplomatic cover.

Iran Is Banking the Conquest — PGSA Institutionalizes Hormuz Tolls Even As the War Ostensibly Ends

The most consequential structural event of the cycle is not the MoU framework or the Beirut strike; it is the May 5 launch of the Persian Gulf Strait Authority (PGSA). Iran via Tasnim/IRIB officially established the body with an info@PGSA.ir email, a mandatory Vessel Information Declaration (ownership, crew nationalities, cargo, voyage plan), a transit-permit regime, and tolls reportedly reaching $2M per vessel (UNVERIFIED quantum, but the framework itself is verified). The statement attributed to Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei calls for “using the leverage of closing the strait” as part of “a strong Iran” regional order. By Day 69, the PGSA is operational: Lloyd’s List (May 7) confirms only ~40 vessels crossed the entire strait the week ending May 3 versus ~120/day pre-war. The U.S. Treasury’s OFAC FAQ confirms such payments are unauthorized for U.S. persons; maritime-law experts say the regime violates UNCLOS’s transit-passage principle.

This is a sovereign claim on an international strait extracted under cover of war. Even if the “Islamabad Accord” lands and Iran agrees to a 30-day MoU window, the PGSA bureaucratic infrastructure has been stood up to claim Hormuz governance as a war-derived right. Iran is converting wartime control into a permanent permit-and-toll regime, the way the 2014 Iraq oil-export disruption created lasting legal frameworks for Kurdish independent crude flows. The dashboard has flagged the “ceasefire that ratifies the conquest” pattern; PGSA is its concrete implementation. Watch the MoU’s Hormuz security clause for whether it dismantles PGSA, accommodates it (de facto recognition with Iranian permit data shared with the IMO), or simply does not mention it — the third option is the most likely and the most strategically consequential, because silence is acquiescence.

The blockade, meanwhile, tightened on Day 68. The F/A-18 from CVN-72 disabling the rudder of the M/T Hasna in the Gulf of Oman is a meaningful capability escalation: aerial gun engagement of a 333-meter tanker is standoff blockade enforcement that does not require destroyer presence in the immediate threat envelope. CENTCOM’s 52 cumulative blockade turnarounds is a counter that has been growing in single-digit increments for the past week despite the Project Freedom pause. France redeploying the Charles de Gaulle to the Red Sea (Macron’s May 6 announcement) signals European willingness to backstop Hormuz freedom of navigation independent of U.S.-led posture, but Macron’s parallel call for both Iran and the U.S. to lift their respective blockades signals Paris views the dual-blockade architecture as the structural problem rather than a U.S. position to be supported. The geometry on Day 69 is: U.S. blockade tightening kinetically; Iranian blockade institutionalized legally; European naval presence emerging as a third pole — not pro-U.S., not pro-Iran. The MoU framework is being negotiated against this three-pole structure, not the two-pole structure that prevailed before May 5.
