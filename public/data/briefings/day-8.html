The Decision to Strike Oil

The Kharg Island strikes represent a significant escalation in targeting philosophy. The initial campaign focused on nuclear, military, and air defense targets. Expanding to economic infrastructure — specifically the facility handling 90% of Iran's oil exports — transforms the conflict from a counter-proliferation operation into an economic warfare campaign. The stated objective of degrading Iran's "ability to fund retaliation" establishes a precedent that the opponent's entire economic base is a legitimate target.

The irony is that destroying Iranian oil export capacity while the Strait of Hormuz is closed has limited immediate military impact — Iran can't export oil through a blockaded strait regardless. The strikes are better understood as ensuring Iran cannot rapidly resume exports if Hormuz reopens, creating long-term economic leverage. But oil markets don't discriminate between cause and effect: any additional supply destruction pushes prices higher, and Brent breaking $100 is a psychological threshold that changes consumer and political behavior.

Information Warfare Dimension

The real risk is not the leaked content but the erosion of confidence in secure communications and the potential for fabricated documents to create false narratives that persist in the information ecosystem.
