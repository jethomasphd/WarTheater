The Islamabad Collapse: 21 Hours to Nowhere

VP Vance arrived at Pakistan’s Nur Khan Airbase Saturday morning, met by FM Ishaq Dar and Chief of Army Staff Field Marshal Asim Munir. Both delegations met separately with PM Shehbaz Sharif before convening. Direct face-to-face negotiations — the highest-level US-Iran meeting since the 1979 revolution — began Saturday afternoon at the Serena Hotel. The US delegation of approximately 300 members was led by Vance, Special Envoy Steve Witkoff, Jared Kushner, and NSA Andrew Baker; Iran’s 70-member delegation was led by Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and FM Abbas Araghchi.

Talks ran continuously through the night, entering a “technical phase involving exchange of written proposals” before collapsing early Sunday morning local time. Vance spoke with Trump a dozen times over the 21-hour period, plus Rubio, Bessent, and CENTCOM’s Adm. Brad Cooper. At his press conference around 5-6 AM local, Vance said the US had been “quite flexible” and “quite accommodating,” and had left a “final and best offer” for Iran to consider. “We need to see an affirmative commitment that they will not seek a nuclear weapon, and they will not seek the tools that would enable them to quickly achieve a nuclear weapon.” He boarded Air Force Two at 7:08 AM.

Five fundamental gaps emerged. The nuclear program: Washington demands Iran abandon all enrichment; Tehran insists on its right to civilian nuclear power. The Strait of Hormuz: Iran demands continued control and toll-collection rights; the US demands unconditional reopening. Turkish media reported Araghchi and Witkoff “nearly came to blows” during these discussions. Lebanon/Hezbollah: Iran’s 10-point proposal makes peace conditional on ending Israeli strikes; the US and Israel refuse to include Lebanon. Sanctions and $6B+ frozen assets. War reparations: Iran demands “full compensation” for war damages. Ghalibaf’s five-point X post blamed Washington: “America has understood our logic and principles, and now it’s time for it to decide whether it can earn our trust or not.”

Trump’s Counter-Blockade: Strategic Flip or Powder Keg?

Within hours of the talks’ collapse, Trump posted on Truth Social: “Effective immediately, the United States Navy, the Finest in the World, will begin the process of BLOCKADING any and all Ships trying to enter, or leave, the Strait of Hormuz.” He ordered the Navy to “seek and interdict every vessel in International Waters that has paid a toll to Iran,” destroy Iranian mines, and warned: “Any Iranian who fires at us, or at peaceful vessels, will be BLOWN TO HELL!” He closed with: “At an appropriate moment, we are fully ‘LOCKED AND LOADED,’ and our Military will finish up the little that is left of Iran!” Axios reports Trump had been discussing the blockade option as a contingency for days.

The strategic logic is to flip Iran’s chokepoint. Rather than let Tehran operate the “Tehran Tollbooth” — charging up to $2 million per vessel via the Larak Island corridor at 15 ships per day — Washington denies all Strait access under US naval control. Lexington Institute’s Rebecca Grant: “It would be very easy for the US Navy to exert complete control over what does and does not go up and down the Strait now.” The historic April 11 transit of USS Frank E. Petersen Jr. (DDG-121) and USS Michael Murphy (DDG-112) — the first US warships through the Strait since the war began — was framed by CENTCOM as establishing “a new passage.” A recorded radio exchange captured the IRGC warning: “This is the last warning,” with the US ship responding: “Passage in accordance with international law. No challenge is intended.”

Two complications loom. First, Iran reportedly lost track of mines it planted, making clearance both essential and dangerous. Second, the countries that have been transiting under Iranian deals — Chinese, Indian, and Pakistani vessels — are precisely those Trump’s interdiction order now targets. Forcing US Navy boarding of a Chinese-flagged tanker in the Gulf of Oman would create a geopolitical crisis far larger than the war itself. Iran’s deputy parliament speaker Ali Nikzad responded: the US “learned that the victorious side is determined by the will of nations and superiority on the battlefield, not by rhetoric on social media.”

There is one historic signal of possible de-escalation. Lebanese Ambassador Nada Hamadeh Moawad and Israeli Ambassador Yechiel Leiter spoke by phone April 11 — the first official Lebanon-Israel contact since 1983. Talks are set for Tuesday, April 15 at the US State Department. But Lebanese PM Nawaf Salam postponed his Washington trip citing “current internal circumstances,” thousands protested in Lebanon against the planned negotiations, and Leiter explicitly stated: “Israel refused to discuss a ceasefire with the Hezbollah terrorist organization.” Five IDF divisions remain deployed in southern Lebanon; Hezbollah resumed rocket fire after the Iran ceasefire, with “fierce clashes” reported in Bint Jbeil.

Analysts describe this as a “strategic pause” rather than capacity loss — a card still on the table if the broader ceasefire collapses after April 22.
