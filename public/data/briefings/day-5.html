The Fordow Question

The confirmation that Fordow's underground facilities likely survived is the most strategically significant development since the war began. The facility, built inside a mountain near Qom and buried under 80+ meters of rock, was specifically designed to withstand aerial bombardment. The GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrator — the heaviest conventional bomb in the US arsenal — was developed partly with Fordow in mind, but the facility's depth exceeds the weapon's verified penetration capability.

If Fordow's centrifuge halls remain operational, the war's stated objective of permanently preventing an Iranian nuclear weapon cannot be verified as achieved through airpower alone. This creates an uncomfortable strategic paradox: the most destructive US air campaign since 2003 may have failed at its central purpose, while succeeding in devastating Iranian military and civilian infrastructure that was never the primary target.

Air Defense Under Pressure

The Al Udeid attack demonstrated that Iran's drone-and-missile saturation strategy can penetrate even the most heavily defended US installation in the region. The combination of slow, cheap Shahed drones to saturate air defense radars and tracking systems, followed by faster ballistic missiles, represents a tactical evolution that maximizes the cost ratio between offense and defense. Each Shahed costs an estimated $20,000-$50,000; each interceptor costs $1-4 million. At 40+ drones per attack, the arithmetic favors the attacker.
