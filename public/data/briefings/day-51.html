Hormuz Whipsaw: Opened April 17, Re-closed April 18, India-Flagged Tankers Hit, Traffic Down 95%

The most consequential operational development since last sync was the 24-hour opening of the Strait of Hormuz followed by Iran’s near-immediate reversal. On April 17 FM Abbas Araghchi declared the strait “fully open” to all commercial vessels via a coordinated Qeshm–Larak routing for the duration of the Lebanon ceasefire, and crude prices dumped 9–15% intraday on the news. Per CNN, only about nine ships actually transited during the opening window. On April 18 the IRGC Navy reversed the decision, declaring the strait closed until the US lifts its blockade: “Approaching the Strait of Hormuz will be considered cooperation with the enemy, and any offending vessel will be targeted.” Iranian gunboats fired on a tanker the same day; an unknown projectile struck a container vessel; and two India-flagged vessels were hit, prompting India to summon Iran’s ambassador. Trump said the US and Iran were jointly working to remove Iranian-laid sea mines in the strait.

The macro impact of the reversal reveals Iran’s residual leverage at comparatively trivial cost. IMF PortWatch clocked only 14 vessels transiting in the prior 24 hours, mostly Iranian-flagged, and the pre-war baseline of ~178 ships/day moving 20–21 mb/d remains down roughly 95%. Polymarket pricing implies a 78.5% probability Hormuz traffic does not normalize by April 30. CENTCOM’s cumulative blockade tally stands at 23 vessels turned back since the blockade began per an April 18 count (Joint Chiefs Chairman Caine had said 13 on April 16); zero have evaded. CENTCOM issued no press releases between April 13 and April 19 — operational messaging has shifted to Pentagon briefings and social media. USS Canberra (LCS-30) was photographed by CENTCOM in the Persian Gulf on April 18, and USS Chief (MCM-14) and USS Pioneer (MCM-9) departed Sasebo, transited Singapore April 8–10, and are inbound to Fifth Fleet to reinforce mine-countermeasures capacity.

The financial gradient is sharp and immediate. War-risk insurance premiums remain elevated at roughly 0.3–1.0%+ of hull value per transit depending on source, with some crowd-sourced trackers citing peaks above 5%; pre-war baseline was 0.05–0.15%. Major insurers (Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, London P&I, American Club) cancelled war-risk coverage effective March 5, and the US Development Finance Corporation is reportedly backstopping Hormuz war-risk cover. Iranian exports paradoxically held near pre-war levels at 1.71 mb/d in April month-to-date per Kpler, generating roughly $4.97 billion in oil revenue in the past month at prices rarely below $90. Iran’s 10-point plan continues to demand Hormuz transit fees ratified in a binding UNSC resolution; the US continues to demand unconditional reopening. Trump’s April 18 threat of “dropping bombs again” collides with Tehran’s simultaneous reversal in a fundamental asymmetry — the US holds overwhelming kinetic and blockade leverage, but Iran retains veto power over the strait at comparatively trivial cost.

The European alternative track pointedly marginalized Washington. Macron: the mission will be “a neutral mission, entirely separate from the belligerents.” Starmer: deployment “as soon as conditions allow,” with roughly 12 countries pledging naval assets.
