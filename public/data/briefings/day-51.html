Hormuz Reclosed: IRGC Gunboats, 20 Tankers Reverse, Parliament Drafts “Strait Security” Fee

The defining event of April 18 was Iran’s reimposition of restrictions on the Strait of Hormuz. At roughly 05:19 ET, AP wires broke that Iran’s Joint Military Command had announced via IRIB that “control of the Strait of Hormuz has returned to its previous state … under strict management and control of the armed forces,” accusing the US of “piracy and maritime robbery” for maintaining its port blockade after Iran had declared the waterway open on Friday. Iranian Navy radio traffic monitored by Bloomberg and OSINT trackers instructed merchant vessels that no ships are allowed to pass through. The “Tehran Tollbooth” has formally mutated: Parliament security committee spokesman Ebrahim Rezaei announced Iran is drafting legislation to replace the informal ~$2 million-per-vessel toll with a statutory charge for “securing the strait,” with US and Israeli military vessels barred entirely. National Security Commission head Ebrahim Azizi confirmed ships now require prior Iranian naval authorization.

The kinetic incidents at sea on April 18 validated the shift. Two IRGC gunboats opened fire on the Indian-flagged VLCC Sanmar Herald (carrying Iraqi crude) despite prior clearance; the ship continued via the Iran-sanctioned Larak Island route. Two other Indian-flagged tankers, Minerva Evropi and Nissos Keros, reversed course after taking fire when attempting the “neutral” transit corridor. A container ship reported rocket damage from an unidentified projectile off the Omani coast (UKMTO advisory); no casualties. Approximately 20 outbound tankers turned around inside the strait, per Kpler/TankerTrackers data cited by WSJ and OSINTtechnical. CENTCOM announced 21 vessels total have been turned back since the blockade began (up from 19 reported April 17); zero have evaded. USS Rafael Peralta (DDG-115) was publicly credited with turning back a merchant vessel on April 17. CENTCOM reports 10,000+ Sailors, Marines, and Airmen aboard 16 warships (11 DDGs, 3 LHD/LPD/LSD, 1 CVN, 1 LCS) plus aircraft enforcing the blockade — Hegseth framed it as “less than 10% of America’s naval power.”

The financial gradient is sharp and immediate. Polymarket odds of Hormuz returning to normal by end of April collapsed from 64% to roughly 32% on the reclosure. War-risk insurance rates settled at 0.8–2.5% of hull value per Gulf transit — down from the March peak of 5% but still 8–20x pre-war rates. JPMorgan estimates ~329 vessels in the Gulf with ~$352B in exposed insured value. Only about 17 vessels transited Hormuz on reference Saturday April 11, vs. ~130/day pre-war; the first VLCC cleared the strait April 14. IEA Director Fatih Birol warned April 16 that Europe has “maybe six weeks or so of jet fuel left” if the strait remains disrupted, calling it the “largest oil supply shock in history.” Blockade costs Iran ~$400M/day in lost revenue, and coercive power peaks precisely as the April 22 ceasefire window closes. Trump’s April 18 insistence that “no sticking points” remain in a deal collides with Tehran’s near-simultaneous reversal — a fundamental asymmetry in which the US holds overwhelming kinetic leverage but Iran retains veto power over the strait at comparatively trivial cost.
