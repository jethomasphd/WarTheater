The Ceasefire That Wasn’t: Fight and Talk

The 15-point ceasefire plan delivered via Pakistan represents the first formal written US framework for ending the conflict. Its precise contents have not been made public, but Iran’s reaction reveals its contours: Tehran’s counter-conditions — closure of all US bases in the Gulf, reparations, a Hormuz transit-fee arrangement, an end to Israeli strikes on Hezbollah, lifting of all sanctions, and preservation of Iran’s missile program — are each individually unacceptable to Washington, let alone collectively. Iran’s ambassador to Pakistan added that “no direct or indirect negotiations have taken place between the two countries so far.”

Yet the plan’s existence changes the diplomatic calculus. Multiple mediation channels are now active simultaneously: the White House is arranging possible in-person talks in Pakistan “this weekend” (two Trump officials confirmed to CNN); Egypt’s FM Abdelatty says Cairo is “ready to host”; Turkey confirmed it is “playing a role passing messages”; China “supports all efforts conducive to easing tensions.” The most likely near-term outcome is a “fight and talk” posture — continued combat operations with a growing rhetorical framework for eventual negotiations — while ground-operation options expand with the 82nd Airborne’s arrival.

Speaker Mike Johnson’s assertion that “Operation Epic Fury is almost done” stands in stark tension with the Pentagon’s confirmation of its largest ground-force deployment since Iraq. Johnson’s comments may reflect political messaging ahead of the $200B supplemental fight more than operational reality. The power plant pause deadline of approximately March 28 remains the next credibility test: if talks produce no visible progress, Trump faces the dilemma of whether to follow through on the energy-infrastructure threat that originally drove oil markets into freefall.

The Ground Force Convergence

The 82nd Airborne’s deployment confirmation transforms the character of the US posture from an air-and-naval campaign to one with explicit ground-operation options. The convergence is significant: two Marine ARGs (Tripoli with 2,200 Marines entering the CENTCOM area of operations around March 25–27, and Boxer with 2,200 more in the Pacific with an April ETA), plus approximately 2,000 82nd Airborne paratroopers, and a division-level command structure under Maj. Gen. Tegtmeier. Secretary Rubio’s congressional testimony that the US may need to “physically secure nuclear material inside Iran” provides the stated rationale.

An Atlantic Council analyst assessed that the current force is “not sufficient for a major invasion nor to hold a single city,” suggesting “limited and targeted operations only.” Kharg Island — five miles offshore, handling 90% of Iran’s oil exports — is the most-discussed objective. The island’s geography makes it amenable to a Marine amphibious or airborne seizure, and its economic significance gives it immense leverage value in any negotiation. However, Rep. Nancy Mace walked out of a classified HASC briefing declaring “I will not support troops on the ground in Iran,” and Rep. Gregory Meeks called for subpoenas of Rubio, Kushner, and Witkoff — indicating that domestic political constraints on ground operations are tightening.

The Energy War: Oil Below $97 and Iran’s Hormuz Gambit

Brent’s 3.5% decline to $96.68 — with an intraday plunge of nearly 7% to $93.45 — represents the largest single-day drop since the war began and brings prices approximately $17 below the $113.41 war-peak reached around March 10–12. The decline was driven entirely by ceasefire-plan headlines; when Iran’s rejection hit at approximately 8 AM ET, prices partially recovered. Goldman Sachs described the current disruption as “the largest shock in decades when measured as a share of global supply.” The IEA’s March report cut global demand growth by 210 kb/d and confirmed 400 million barrels released from emergency reserves.

Iran’s letter to the UN and IMO announcing that “non-hostile vessels” may transit the Strait of Hormuz after coordination — with transit fees confirmed at $2 million per vessel — is a calculated hybrid between continued closure and partial reopening. It signals willingness to de-escalate the maritime dimension while maintaining leverage: US and Israeli vessels are explicitly excluded, and the fee structure monetizes Iran’s de facto control. Whether this letter represents a genuine step toward reopening or merely diplomatic theater remains to be seen. US gas prices held at approximately $3.96 per gallon nationally — up 10 consecutive weeks, $1.02 in March alone, and 41% year-to-date. California reached $5.62 per gallon.

The UN Human Rights Council held an urgent debate condemning Iran’s attacks on Gulf neighbors, while a second debate on the Minab school strike (168 children killed February 28) is scheduled for March 27.
