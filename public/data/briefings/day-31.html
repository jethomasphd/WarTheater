Ground Operations: The Next Phase

The Washington Post’s Dan Lamothe reported that the Pentagon is actively planning “weeks of ground operations” inside Iran, marking a potential transformation of the campaign from a standoff air and naval war into something far more expansive and politically fraught. The 82nd Airborne Division’s 1st Brigade Combat Team, headquarters element, and division enablers are deploying to the region, with 7,000–8,000 additional troops in transit. This brings total US ground forces in the broader theater to their highest level since the early months of the Iraq War.

The most closely watched contingency is a potential seizure of Kharg Island — Iran’s primary oil export terminal, responsible for roughly 90% of the country’s crude exports before the war. Senator Lindsey Graham compared the potential operation to Iwo Jima, underscoring both its strategic significance and its expected difficulty. Iran has fortified the island with anti-ship missiles, minefields, and an estimated 2,000–3,000 IRGC defenders. The convergence of amphibious forces — the Tripoli ARG now operational, the Boxer ARG transiting from the Pacific — provides the lift capacity for such an operation.

The timing is not coincidental. Trump’s April 6 deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz creates a binary escalation trigger: either diplomacy produces a breakthrough in the next seven days, or the campaign shifts to a ground-intensive phase targeting the very infrastructure Trump has threatened to “obliterate.” The entry of Iraqi PMF forces into Iran via Khorramshahr adds a new dimension — any US ground operation would now face not only IRGC defenders but potentially Shia militia forces with extensive urban combat experience from the ISIS campaign.

The Diplomatic Window

The Islamabad four-nation summit represents the most structured diplomatic effort since the war began. Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt — collectively representing the major Sunni-majority powers with relationships on both sides — have assembled to broker what PM Sharif called a “realistic pathway to de-escalation.” Iran’s decision to allow 20 Pakistani-flagged vessels through the Strait of Hormuz is a deliberate confidence-building measure, signaling to Islamabad that Tehran takes the mediation seriously while maintaining its blockade on Western-allied shipping.

The negotiating positions remain far apart. The US 15-point plan — demanding zero enrichment, dismantlement of nuclear sites, suspension of the ballistic missile program, curbing proxy support, and immediate Hormuz reopening — was flatly rejected by Tehran. Iran’s counter-proposal demands a complete halt to strikes, mechanisms preventing future attacks, guaranteed reparations, and international recognition of sovereignty over the Strait. Yet the fact that counter-proposals are being exchanged at all represents progress from the first three weeks of mutual maximalism.

VP Vance has been designated lead negotiator alongside Witkoff and Kushner, with Secretary Rubio stating that military objectives could be completed in “weeks not months.” This framing — suggesting the military campaign has a defined endpoint — may be aimed at creating political space for negotiations. The critical question is whether April 6 functions as a genuine deadline or a movable goalpost. Trump has already extended it once. But his Truth Social rhetoric about obliterating infrastructure suggests less flexibility this time — the public commitment makes another extension politically costly.
