The Houthi Escalation: A Third Front and the Dual Chokepoint Nightmare

The Houthis’ entry into the war was the most anticipated and most feared escalation since the conflict’s early days. For 28 days, analysts debated why Ansar Allah — which had demonstrated significant strike capability during the Red Sea crisis of 2023-24 — remained dormant despite their patron’s existential struggle. The INSS attributed restraint to economic pressures and fear of US/Israeli strikes on the port city of Hodeidah. The Soufan Center warned that Houthi activation would serve as a “force multiplier” for Tehran by further depleting US and Israeli air defenses.

The missile barrage itself — targeting the Beersheba/Dimona area in southern Israel — is less significant than what it portends. If the Houthis follow through on their threat to close Bab al-Mandeb, the consequences would be extraordinary. Saudi Arabia has been rerouting crude via the East-West Pipeline to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, which has become the primary bypass for the Hormuz closure. Approximately 30 tankers are currently staged near Yanbu, all within Houthi strike range. A simultaneous closure of both Hormuz and Bab al-Mandeb would eliminate the two primary chokepoints for Middle Eastern energy exports — an unprecedented scenario that would leave only the Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline through Turkey and the ADCOP pipeline through Fujairah as viable export routes, with a combined throughput of roughly 1.3 million barrels per day against pre-war flows of 20+ million.

Markets will react to this news Monday. Oil prices, already at $112.57 Brent, could reprice sharply higher if Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping materialize over the weekend. Goldman Sachs estimates a $14–18/bbl geopolitical risk premium is already embedded in current prices; a dual chokepoint closure would push that premium significantly higher.
