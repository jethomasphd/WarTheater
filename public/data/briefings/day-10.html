The Verification Paradox

The IAEA's loss of monitoring access creates the conflict's deepest strategic paradox. The war was launched to prevent Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons. Iran has now withdrawn from the NPT, expelled inspectors, and the physical damage to known facilities has been assessed as incomplete (Fordow likely survived). The IAEA cannot verify the status of nuclear material — meaning the international community cannot confirm whether the war achieved its objective, and Iran retains the knowledge, materials, and potentially the facilities to reconstitute its program.

This is the "Iraq WMD" problem in reverse: in 2003, the US invaded to destroy weapons that did not exist. In 2026, the US struck to destroy a program that demonstrably existed — but cannot verify that the destruction was complete, and has eliminated the monitoring regime that would provide early warning of reconstitution.

Supply Chain Cascade

The conflict's economic impact is spreading beyond energy markets. The Baltic Dry Index — a proxy for global shipping demand and capacity — spiked 35% as available vessels are absorbed by longer routing around Africa. Semiconductor-grade neon shortages could impact chip manufacturing within weeks. The cascading effects mirror the COVID-era supply chain disruptions but with a different cause: geographic chokepoint closure rather than demand shock.
