Trump’s “Shoot-and-Kill” ROE and the Blockade Accelerant: CENTCOM to 31 Vessels

The defining kinetic move of Day 55 was Trump’s Truth Social order directing the US Navy to “shoot and kill any boat” laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz. It is the most significant rules-of-engagement change since the April 13 blockade: where the earlier order authorized interdiction, the new instruction authorizes pre-emptive lethal force against any mine-laying craft in international waters. The White House framed it narrowly as protection for commercial shipping and as consistent with the indefinite Iran ceasefire. Iran has not yet responded publicly to the ROE, but it raises the risk that a routine IRGC small-boat sortie is met with US 5-inch main-battery or Sea Spear fires, and that a kinetic incident could then cascade back into the air and ground fronts that have been paused since 8 PM ET April 7.

The blockade itself accelerated. The Pentagon confirmed US naval forces boarded another “sanctioned stateless vessel” carrying Iranian crude in the Indian Ocean overnight Apr 22–23. Reuters separately reported at least three Iranian-flagged tankers intercepted in Asian waters on Apr 23 — redirected from positions near India, Malaysia, and Sri Lanka. CENTCOM’s running blockade scorecard rose to 31 commercial vessels turned back since April 13 (up from 28 on Apr 21 and 23 on Apr 18), with zero having broken through. The operation now involves 10,000+ soldiers, 17 warships, and 100+ aircraft. Gen. Caine (JCS Chairman) reaffirmed INDOPACOM Adm. Paparo will “actively pursue any Iranian-flagged vessel or any vessel attempting to provide material support to Iran,” and in the same remarks warned of “finite levels” of munitions being expended — consistent with the munitions-depletion picture FPRI/RUSI/CSIS have documented since mid-March.

Congress came back into the loop. The Senate defeated Senator Tammy Baldwin’s war-powers resolution 55–46 — the fifth such failed attempt since the war began. Most Democrats (plus Rand Paul) voted for it; most Republicans (plus John Fetterman) voted against; Mark Warner and two others did not vote. The statutory 60-day War Powers clock expires around May 1. Separately, the Senate moved forward on a $70 billion ICE/Border Patrol funding package. Trump told reporters there is “no time frame” on the war and rejected midterm-election political framings; White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said he has “not set a firm deadline to receive an Iranian proposal.” Pakistan’s Pakistan’s mediation bandwidth, which produced the first direct US-Iran face-to-face since 1979 for Round 1, remains the key non-kinetic variable — Islamabad Round 2 collapsed on Apr 22, and no rescheduling has been announced.

Iran’s “Tehran Tollbooth” Books First Revenue; Qatar’s 20-for-20 Compromise Blocked

The structural development of Day 55 is that the “Tehran Tollbooth” has moved from informal IRGC practice to institutional revenue stream. Deputy Parliament Speaker Hamid Reza Haji Babaei announced via Fars News on Apr 23: “The first transit toll revenue from the Strait of Hormuz has been deposited into the Central Bank account.” Senior MP Alireza Salimi told Tasnim the fees “are currently being deposited into a unified account and the treasury,” varying by cargo and risk level, with some transits paid in Chinese yuan. The treasury accounting matters. It converts what began as a rumored ~$2M/vessel Larak Island corridor fee into a sovereignty-grade claim, gives the Iranian state a war-financing line in defiance of US sanctions, and hardens Iran’s negotiating position by reframing Hormuz as a revenue asset rather than a military chokepoint.

Qatar’s 20-for-20 compromise died Apr 23. Doha had proposed allowing 20 Iranian vessels to transit Hormuz in exchange for 20 Arab Gulf ships as a limited confidence-building measure. The proposal was reportedly blocked by Iranian officials including FM Abbas Araghchi, who publicly restated that “blockading Iranian ports is an act of war.” Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf said Iran would not reopen the strait while the US naval blockade remained in place, calling the blockade a “blatant violation” of the ceasefire. President Masoud Pezeshkian said Tehran seeks “dialogue and agreement” but blamed US “breach of commitments, blockade and threats” for the stalled talks. The Qatari blocking leaves no visible off-ramp short of lifting the blockade, which the White House has ruled out. Xi Jinping separately told Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman on Apr 21 that the strait “should remain open to normal navigation,” a formulation that implicitly pressures both Washington and Tehran.

The three-carrier posture is unchanged from the April 20 USNI Fleet Tracker baseline (no new tracker was published on Apr 23). Ford (CVN-78) / CSG-12 (CVW-8 embarked with VFA-31/37/87/213, VAQ-142, VAW-124) is operating in the central Red Sea after transiting Suez southbound on April 16. The USS George H.W. Bush (CVN-77) / CSG-10 (with USS Mason, USS Donald Cook, USS Ross) is transiting the Horn of Africa; on arrival, CENTCOM will have three carriers simultaneously in theater. The USS Theodore Roosevelt (CVN-71) / CSG-9 departed San Diego on April 15 with new Raytheon Coyote 8-cell counter-UAS launchers (routine 3rd Fleet ops). USS George Washington (CVN-73) is in port Yokosuka; the Boxer ARG (LHD-4) is near Guam with 11th MEU; USS Kearsarge (LHD-3) has arrived Houston for Fleet Week. The UK and France concluded a two-day 50-country Hormuz protection conference at an RAF base north of London on Apr 22–23 to plan a multinational mine-clearance mission, with British officials cautioning any operation will only commence after a “sustained ceasefire.” The Pentagon briefed the House Armed Services Committee in a classified session on Apr 21 that clearing Strait of Hormuz mines could take up to six months after the war ends; DIA internal assessment range is 1–6 months.

Humanitarian & Lebanon: Al-Akhbar Journalist Killed, UNDP Warns 32M Into Poverty, Israel-Lebanon Talks Open at White House

Southern Lebanon took the day’s sharpest humanitarian hit. Lebanese journalist Amal Khalil of Al-Akhbar was killed in an Israeli strike on the village of al-Tiri; in total four to five people were killed in southern Lebanon across Apr 22–23. Lebanese Prime Minister Nawaf Salam called the strike a “war crime”; the UN and the Committee to Protect Journalists both condemned the killing. Separately, the IDF Commando Brigade killed two armed Hezbollah operatives near Aynata (adjacent to Bint Jbeil). Channel 12 reported that the Jerusalem assessment “is increasingly pointing to a resumption of fighting with Iran drawing closer.” Lebanon’s cumulative toll, per Al Jazeera’s Apr 23 tracker, stands at 2,294 killed (PBS reported 2,290 on Apr 22) with at least 7,658 wounded as of Apr 21. Internal Hezbollah sources admit more than 1,000 fighters killed; the IDF claim is 1,700. (A Day 54 file entry cited Lebanon’s disaster-management unit at 2,454 killed; the lower 2,294 figure reflects the MoPH baseline excluding certain Hezbollah combat losses, and the two series diverge.)

On the diplomatic track, the second round of direct Israel–Lebanon ambassadors’ talks opened in Washington, moved from the State Department to the White House. Israel was represented by Ambassador Yechiel Leiter, Lebanon by Ambassador Nada Hamadeh Moawad; Secretary Rubio was expected to join for part of the session, and the US added Ambassador Mike Huckabee to the US delegation. The Lebanese position was unchanged: no deal without a full Israeli withdrawal. Retired BGen Mark Kimmitt told Al Jazeera the talks had “functional flaws.” The Iran casualty picture remained frozen since the April 7 ceasefire: 3,375 killed per Iranian authorities via Al Jazeera with 26,500+ injured; 3,636 documented by HRANA (1,701 civilians incl. 254 children + 1,221 military + 714 unclassified); HRANA estimated ≥15% of casualties are under age 18 as of March 23. The structural humanitarian number of Day 55 came from the UN Development Program. De Croo warned that the peak of food insecurity is still ahead given disrupted fertilizer flows. At sea, the International Transport Workers’ Federation has received 1,900 requests for assistance from seafarers stranded in the Persian Gulf since the war began; the IMO condemned the ongoing ship attacks and seizures. Kuwaiti-American journalist Ahmed Shihab-Eldin, held 52 days in Kuwait after posting Iran war videos, was acquitted of all charges. Infrastructure damage in Iran remained the baseline shock: 442 health facilities damaged (UNICEF, affecting services for 10M including 2.2M children), 763 schools damaged (IRCS), 115,193 civilian housing units damaged, and 25 Iranian hospitals damaged with 9 out of service per NPR/Iranian officials (March 13).
