The War Powers Crisis: From Theory to Text on Day 64

Day 64 takes the WPR confrontation from speculation to formal record. Trump’s May 1 letters to Speaker Johnson and Senate Pres Pro Tem Grassley assert “hostilities that began on Saturday, February 28, 2026, have terminated” — not that the clock is paused (Hegseth’s SASC theory), but that the predicate fact triggering the clock no longer obtains. Trump separately, in spoken remarks, called the WPR “totally unconstitutional”: a belt-and-suspenders position. The legal vulnerability of “terminated” is the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports, which most international-law scholars classify as an act of war — and 45 vessels redirected since Apr 13 (CENTCOM May 1 figure) is uncomfortably difficult to characterize as the absence of hostilities. Yet the political cost of resuming kinetic operations is now bound up with the legal claim: any Cooper-recommended strike post-May 1 falsifies the “terminated” assertion in real time.

Senate math has shifted only at the margin. The Apr 30 vote failed 50–47 (sixth attempt) — tighter than prior votes, but still short of the 51 needed to advance. The single most important political signal is Sen. Susan Collins’ first crossover, on the basis that the deadline “is not a suggestion; it is a requirement.” Watch for additional Republican defectors — Murkowski, Young, Cramer, Tillis — on a future AUMF rather than a WPR resolution. Sen. Lisa Murkowski (R-AK) has said she will introduce an AUMF when the Senate returns from recess the week of May 11; Sen. Mike Rounds (R-SD) expects Trump to seek a statutory 30-day extension. Congress is in a week-long recess; no Democratic lawsuit has been filed as of May 2, despite Time’s Apr 28 scoop suggesting one was being explored.

The next 7–14 days are now scripted around three institutional escape hatches: (a) an OLC opinion drop reasserting Article II “inherent authority” — the most consequential single document the administration could publish; (b) a Murkowski AUMF that flushes additional GOP defectors; (c) a Democratic lawsuit that forces a court to engage the WPR on its merits, an engagement courts have declined since the statute’s 1973 enactment over Nixon’s veto. No prior administration has formally argued the 60-day clock can be terminated by a ceasefire while a naval blockade continues. The Trump administration’s theory is novel; whether it survives contact with a court that has, until now, declined to engage is the constitutional question of the war.
