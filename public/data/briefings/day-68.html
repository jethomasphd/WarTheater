Attribution Is Decaying as a Category — Three Iranian-Attributed Incidents in Five Days Disputed

Day 68 inherits a structural problem that crystallized over the past five days: the OSINT analyst can no longer treat single-source Iranian-state-media attributions as reliable. Three Iranian-attributed incidents in five days have been disputed: the Day-66 Jask warship strike (Fars claim, CENTCOM denial); the Day-67 fake “Hatami” warning to U.S. carriers (Fars correction; CNN traced to a fake X account); and the Day-67 UAE second-day attack (UAE MoD claim, IRGC explicit denial via Fars: “forces have not carried out any missile or drone operations… absolutely denied and devoid of any truth”). What does not show the same dispute pattern is the May 4 UAE attack itself, where 12 BMs + 3 CMs + 4 UAVs were reportedly engaged and ADNOC’s “Barakah” tanker was struck — Iran has not formally denied that strike.

Three explanations are operationally plausible. First, genuine command-and-control fragmentation in IRGC: Iran’s Mojtaba Khamenei is still in hiding (NYT Apr 23: “disfiguring” facial burns; struggles to speak), and Axios sourcing from Day 59 reports Araghchi told Pakistani/Egyptian/Turkish/Qatari mediators “there’s no consensus inside the Iranian leadership.” Second, deliberate disinformation by an IRGC information-warfare cell that is operating on its own ROE. Third, third-party (Houthi, Iraqi PMU, or hybrid) action that Iran is explicitly disclaiming to preserve negotiating space. The Day-68 implication is that all three vectors are still live and not mutually exclusive. Operationally, the analyst should treat all single-source Iranian-state-media attributions as MEDIUM confidence at best, and treat Iranian denials as legitimate signals rather than boilerplate.

This matters for MOU drafting. If the “Islamabad Accord” package contains provisions tying Iran to non-state proxy actions in the Gulf, Tehran will likely reject those provisions on the grounds that it cannot bind actors it does not control. Conversely, if the package allows Iran to disclaim attribution on a case-by-case basis, every future incident becomes deniable — which functionally erodes the deal’s deterrent value before the ink dries. The dashboard should treat the next 72 hours of UAE / Hormuz incident reporting as a stress test: if the dispute pattern holds (Iran denies, UAE / U.S. attribute), the underlying attribution architecture is breaking down structurally. If a single high-confidence multi-source attribution of an Iranian-state action emerges, it will be treated as a deliberate Iranian signal that command authority has been re-consolidated — and that the MOU is closer to delivery than the bifurcated administration messaging suggests.
