The Mediation Race: Pakistan, Egypt, and the Islamabad Channel

The most significant diplomatic development of Day 25 was not a statement or a summit but a quiet repositioning of mediating powers. Pakistan has stepped forward as the leading intermediary, with army chief General Asim Munir speaking directly to Trump and ISI chief engaging with US envoys Witkoff and Kushner. According to Axios and multiple sources, an Islamabad meeting is being arranged that would bring Iranian Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf face-to-face with American negotiators — and possibly Vice President Vance. If it materializes, this would be the first direct contact between the two sides since the war began.

Pakistan’s positioning makes strategic sense. It shares a 959-kilometer border with Iran, hosts millions of Afghan refugees who could be displaced by the conflict, and maintains relationships with both Tehran and Washington. The ISI has historically served as an intermediary in sensitive regional negotiations. Egypt is pursuing a parallel track, with officials telling NPR they are laying groundwork for a 30-60 day ceasefire framework. Turkey’s FM Fidan conducted a burst of calls on March 22 with Araghchi, Egyptian FM Abdelatty, and Witkoff. Oman continues passing messages on Hormuz.

The fundamental obstacle remains: the US shared what it calls a “15-point list” via Pakistan, but CNN’s two regional sources say it is unclear whether Iran has agreed to any of the points. Ghalibaf publicly denied all negotiations on X, calling reports “fake news” and “market manipulation.” Senior Iranian FM officials told CBS they have “received the points” and are “reviewing” them — a more nuanced position than the public denials suggest. The five-day power plant pause deadline of approximately March 28 creates urgency: if talks fail to produce visible progress, Trump faces a credibility dilemma on whether to follow through on his threat.
