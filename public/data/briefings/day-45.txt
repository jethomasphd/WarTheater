THE BLOCKADE GOES LIVE: TWO ORDERS, ONE STRAIT
=========================================================

Trump's Truth Social declaration — "Effective immediately, the United States Navy, the Finest in the World, will begin the process of BLOCKADING any and all Ships trying to enter, or leave, the Strait of Hormuz" — was followed within hours by a CENTCOM operational order substantially narrower in scope. The Navy's actual mission, as CENTCOM described it, applies only to ships entering or exiting Iranian ports; freedom of navigation for vessels transiting the Strait to and from non-Iranian ports remains protected. The gap between the two statements is not cosmetic. Trump's version, taken at face value, would force US Navy boarding of Chinese, Indian, and Pakistani-flagged vessels that have been paying Iran's $2M toll via the Larak Island corridor — a geopolitical crisis far larger than the war itself. CENTCOM's version confines the fight to Iranian commerce and leaves the larger international merchant fleet alone. Al Jazeera openly flagged the contradiction.

Iran's response was immediate and total. The IRGC warned that military vessels approaching the Strait "will be considered a violation of the ceasefire and will be met with severe force." Iran's armed forces called the blockade "an illegal act amounting to piracy." IRGC-controlled outlets declared: "Security in the Persian Gulf and the Sea of Oman is either for everyone or for NO ONE." Senior military adviser Mohsen Rezaei dismissed the blockade as "tweets and imaginary plans" and pointed to Iran's "major untouched levers." The IRGC coupled its Hormuz warning with an explicit threat to the Bab el-Mandeb Strait on the Yemeni side: "if Trump acts on Hormuz, he will also lose the Bab el-Mandeb." Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf, who six days earlier had led Iran's delegation to Islamabad, posted Washington gas station prices to social media with a taunt: "Enjoy the current pump figures... Soon you'll be nostalgic for $4-$5 gas."

International coalition support for enforcement has been notably thin. The United Kingdom explicitly declined to participate — PM Starmer: Britain "not getting dragged into the war." Australia said it hasn't been asked. EU Commission President von der Leyen called freedom of navigation of "paramount importance" and noted EU fossil fuel import costs have increased by €22 billion over 44 days. China urged both sides not to reignite war. Spain's defense minister called the blockade "one more episode in this whole downward spiral." The only vocal endorsement came from Netanyahu, who warned the ceasefire "could end at any moment" — a signal that Tel Aviv, not Washington, sees the blockade as strategic cover for renewed Lebanon operations.

The ceasefire on the Iran front has now held for approximately 144 hours — zero confirmed US/Israeli strikes on Iranian territory and zero Iranian retaliation strikes on April 12-13.
