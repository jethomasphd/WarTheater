Project Freedom is Calibrated, Not Maximalist — And Iran’s Response Is Asymmetric By Design

Trump’s chosen escalation path is calibrated rather than maximalist. Per Axios reporting confirmed by multiple sources, CENTCOM Cdr Adm. Cooper presented Trump on Apr 30 with a “forced-entry” plan to fight through the Strait with full destruction of any Iranian response and resumption of war. Trump rejected it in favor of Project Freedom — more limited (15,000 troops, 100+ aircraft, 2 DDGs in Gulf, “guide” not “escort”), but with updated rules of engagement that authorize strikes against immediate threats. A White House source described it as “the beginning of a process that could lead to a confrontation.” Markets are correctly pricing the escalation risk: VIX +4.4% intraday pre-mkt, Brent +5.8%, 30Y Treasury yield through 5%. The fact that defense ETFs closed down on the day (ITA -0.51%, XAR -1.30%) while energy was the only S&P sector green tells the political-economy story: investors believe the calibration holds, but they also believe defense valuations are stretched and supplemental-funding uncertainty is real.

Iran’s response signal is asymmetric and escalatory by design. Rather than directly engage U.S. warships, Tehran (a) targeted Gulf-state energy infrastructure with apparent maximum political effect / minimum U.S.-trigger calibration — an empty ADNOC tanker yields zero ADNOC casualties but maximum sovereignty insult; the Fujairah Oil Industry Zone strike injures three Indian nationals while damaging the very pipeline that gives Abu Dhabi its Hormuz-bypass option — and (b) ran a clear information operation about a U.S. warship strike that drove a 5% Brent spike before being denied. This is the IRGC’s stated “impossible military operation or a bad deal” messaging operationalized. Watch for further pattern: empty-cargo / state-symbolic vessel targeting; UAE / Saudi / Bahrain energy-infrastructure pressure; sustained IO tempo on warship strikes.

Tehran’s second move is structural rather than kinetic. The IRGC-N’s newly released maritime control map — expanding declared control to encompass Fujairah, Khorfakkan, and Umm Al Quwain coasts — is paired with Parliament’s pending 12-article Hormuz sovereignty law (rial payment, 20% cargo seizure, hostile-country permit-and-reparations regime). The combined posture is not a tactical demand but a legal architecture engineered to outlast any near-term ceasefire. Parliament’s Ebrahim Azizi explicitly framed any U.S. “interference in the new maritime regime” as a ceasefire violation. The 14-point proposal’s “new mechanism for the Strait of Hormuz” demand is the diplomatic surface of this same campaign. Watch for the Majlis chamber vote on recess return: if adopted, it becomes the single most consequential structural change to the maritime law-of-the-sea regime since UNCLOS.
