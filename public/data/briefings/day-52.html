Touska Day: USS Spruance Seizes Iranian M/V Touska in Gulf of Oman — First Major Kinetic Blockade Enforcement

The defining operational event of Day 52 is the April 19 seizure of the Iranian-flagged M/V Touska by USS Spruance (DDG-111), an Arleigh Burke-class destroyer of the 5th Fleet. The Touska — a roughly 900-foot container ship that had departed Port Klang bound for Bandar Abbas at 17 knots — was intercepted in the Gulf of Oman / North Arabian Sea. After a roughly six-hour standoff, Spruance fired 5-inch MK45 rounds into the Touska’s engine room, disabling propulsion. Marines of the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit then rappelled from two MH-60S Seahawks off USS Tripoli (LHA-7) to conduct a visit, board, search, and seizure (VBSS), taking the ship into custody. Trump announced the seizure first on Truth Social; Iran’s foreign ministry condemned it as “armed piracy.” Sources include CENTCOM’s statement, CNN, CNBC, Axios, the Jerusalem Post, and The Aviationist.

Touska materially raises the kinetic floor of the blockade. CENTCOM now reports 25 commercial vessels redirected since April 13 — up from Gen. Caine’s April 16 count of 13 and the April 18 tally of 23 — though Touska is the first case of a US destroyer using main gun fire against an Iranian-flagged hull rather than simply turning vessels around. The seizure happened during the ceasefire, at sea rather than on Iranian territory, which lets the administration argue it is consistent with the two-week truce framework while still demonstrating escalation dominance ahead of the April 21 expiry clock (~20:00 ET Tuesday). Kinetic events in the preceding 48 hours — IRGC gunboats firing on VLCC Sanmar Herald, CMA CGM Everglade taking rocket damage off Oman, cruise ship Mein Schiff 4 threatened on VHF, India summoning Iran’s ambassador after two India-flagged vessels were hit — frame Touska as one node in a broader spiral rather than an isolated interdiction.

The parallel legislative move out of Tehran reveals how Iran intends to institutionalize its leverage. On April 20 MP Mohammad-Reza Rezaei-Kouchi announced a parliamentary draft law that would formalize the long-rumored “Tehran Tollbooth” — barring “hostile” country vessels from transiting the Strait of Hormuz and imposing statutory tolls on others, replacing the informal ~$2M-per-vessel transit charge with codified architecture. Hormuz throughput remains more than 90% below the pre-war baseline, with roughly 10 mb/d disrupted; UKMTO transit counts show 8 vessels April 9, 2 on April 10, and 4 on April 11. VLCC spot rates peaked at $423,736/day in March per LSEG record data. The structural asymmetry remains intact: the US can interdict Iranian hulls and maintain blockade enforcement, while Iran can shut or selectively gate the strait at comparatively trivial cost, and the cumulative cost of that standoff now runs on three clocks — Touska’s legal custody, the April 21 ceasefire expiry, and the tollbooth law’s legislative timeline.
