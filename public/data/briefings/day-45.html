The Blockade Goes Live: Two Orders, One Strait

The operational order that went out from CENTCOM this afternoon is not the order Trump announced on Truth Social yesterday. Trump declared that the Navy would “begin the process of BLOCKADING any and all Ships trying to enter, or leave, the Strait of Hormuz,” and would “seek and interdict every vessel in International Waters that has paid a toll to Iran.” CENTCOM’s actual statement, hours later, narrowed the aperture dramatically: the blockade applies only to ships entering or exiting Iranian ports, and “will not impede freedom of navigation for vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz to and from non-Iranian ports.” Al Jazeera’s correspondent flagged the “conflicting information” between the two statements. The gap matters enormously: Trump’s version would have forced confrontation with Chinese, Indian, and Pakistani tankers operating under Iranian-permitted transit; CENTCOM’s version protects that commerce and narrows the zone of conflict to Iranian flag traffic.

Iran’s response has been uniformly hostile and uniformly escalatory. The IRGC declared that any military vessel approaching the Strait would be treated as a ceasefire violator and met with “severe force.” Iran’s armed forces called the blockade “an illegal act amounting to piracy.” Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf posted Washington gas station prices on social media, taunting: “Enjoy the current pump figures… Soon you’ll be nostalgic for $4-$5 gas.” Senior military adviser Mohsen Rezaei said Iran’s forces have “major untouched levers” and would not be coerced by “tweets and imaginary plans.” IRGC: “Security in the Persian Gulf and the Sea of Oman is either for everyone or for NO ONE”; “NO PORT in the region will be safe.” The IRGC also warned that if Trump acts on Hormuz, “he will also lose the Bab el-Mandeb Strait” — the first explicit threat to reactivate the Houthi chokepoint since the ceasefire.

The coalition Trump hoped to assemble did not materialize. The United Kingdom explicitly declined to participate, with PM Starmer stating Britain is “not getting dragged into the war.” Australia said it had not been asked. EU Commission President von der Leyen called freedom of navigation “paramount” and noted EU fossil fuel import costs have increased by €22 billion in 44 days. Spain’s defense minister called the blockade “one more episode in this whole downward spiral.” A Wall Street Journal assessment adds a harder edge: 60%+ of the IRGC’s naval fleet tasked with Hormuz patrol remains intact — directly contradicting the White House claim of 97% mine elimination and 150 ships sunk. Lexington Institute’s Rebecca Grant puts the optimistic case: “It would be very easy for the US Navy to exert complete control over what does and does not go up and down the Strait now.” The pessimistic case is simpler: the IRGC still has ships, still has mines, and has now told Washington in writing that it intends to use them.

There is one historic signal of possible de-escalation.
