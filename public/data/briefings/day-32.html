The Energy War Enters a New Phase

The Al Salmi attack at Dubai port marks a qualitative escalation in Iran’s asymmetric campaign. Previous attacks targeted vessels in the open waters of the Strait of Hormuz; striking a VLCC at a major commercial port demonstrates that no maritime facility in the region is beyond Iran’s reach, even as its conventional navy lies at the bottom of the Arabian Sea. The risk of a major oil spill at Dubai — one of the world’s busiest shipping hubs — adds environmental catastrophe to the economic disruption.

The energy picture is dire by any historical measure. The IEA described the disruption as the worst in the history of the global oil market, with Gulf producers having lost at least 10 million barrels per day of export capacity by March 12. Only 89 ships crossed the Strait of Hormuz between March 1–15 versus a normal rate of 100–135 per day. Approximately 3,200 ships (4% of global tonnage) remain stranded with 500 waiting outside the Gulf. Insurance has become effectively prohibitive at 5–10% of hull value per transit — a 100x increase from pre-war levels.

The supply response remains inadequate. The U.S. SPR sits at approximately 345 million barrels. OFAC’s General License U, temporarily lifting sanctions on ~140 million barrels of Iranian crude at sea, expires April 19. Saudi Arabia is diverting oil via its East-West pipeline to Yanbu, and the UAE is using the ADCOP pipeline to Fujairah, but effective bypass capacity stands at roughly 3.5 million barrels per day against a 16.5 million barrel per day shortfall. If the Houthis close Bab al-Mandeb, even the Yanbu bypass becomes vulnerable — 70–75% of pipeline exports must transit through that chokepoint.

Diplomatic Stalemate — But an Off-Ramp Emerges

The Islamabad diplomatic track represents the most structured mediation effort yet, but the distance between the parties remains vast. The U.S. 15-point plan demanded complete dismantlement of enrichment capability, removal of all enriched uranium, ballistic missile limits, and cessation of proxy support. Iran’s five counter-conditions demanded an end to all strikes, compensation for damages, international sovereignty recognition over Hormuz, and security guarantees. Neither side has shown willingness to move significantly toward the other.

Yet an unexpected development could reshape the strategic calculus. The Wall Street Journal reported that Trump told aides he may end the war without securing Strait of Hormuz control — and the White House confirmed that reopening the strait is “not a core objective.” If true, this fundamentally redefines the war’s endgame. The original casus belli was Iran’s closure of Hormuz; abandoning that objective while maintaining strikes creates a war without a clear political endpoint, which historically produces protracted and costly conflicts.

China’s role deserves close attention. Beijing abstained (rather than vetoed) on UNSC Resolution 2817 condemning Iran, while quietly stockpiling approximately 1.2 billion barrels of oil reserves (3–4 months of supply). China has conveyed support for Pakistan’s mediation role. The two Chinese container ships that transited Hormuz on March 30 — the first cargo vessels since the war began — suggest Beijing is leveraging its special relationship with Tehran to maintain supply while the rest of the world suffers disruption.
