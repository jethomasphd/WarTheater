Trump’s “Shoot-and-Kill” ROE and the Blockade Accelerant: CENTCOM to 31 Vessels

The defining kinetic move of Day 55 was Trump’s Truth Social order directing the US Navy to “shoot and kill any boat” laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz. It is the most significant rules-of-engagement change since the April 13 blockade: where the earlier order authorized interdiction, the new instruction authorizes pre-emptive lethal force against any mine-laying craft in international waters. The White House framed it narrowly as protection for commercial shipping and as consistent with the indefinite Iran ceasefire. Iran has not yet responded publicly to the ROE, but it raises the risk that a routine IRGC small-boat sortie is met with US 5-inch main-battery or Sea Spear fires, and that a kinetic incident could then cascade back into the air and ground fronts that have been paused since 8 PM ET April 7.

The blockade itself accelerated. The Pentagon confirmed US naval forces boarded another “sanctioned stateless vessel” carrying Iranian crude in the Indian Ocean overnight Apr 22–23. Reuters separately reported at least three Iranian-flagged tankers intercepted in Asian waters on Apr 23 — redirected from positions near India, Malaysia, and Sri Lanka. CENTCOM’s running blockade scorecard rose to 31 commercial vessels turned back since April 13 (up from 28 on Apr 21 and 23 on Apr 18), with zero having broken through. The operation now involves 10,000+ soldiers, 17 warships, and 100+ aircraft. Gen. Caine (JCS Chairman) reaffirmed INDOPACOM Adm. Paparo will “actively pursue any Iranian-flagged vessel or any vessel attempting to provide material support to Iran,” and in the same remarks warned of “finite levels” of munitions being expended — consistent with the munitions-depletion picture FPRI/RUSI/CSIS have documented since mid-March.

Congress came back into the loop. The Senate defeated Senator Tammy Baldwin’s war-powers resolution 55–46 — the fifth such failed attempt since the war began. Most Democrats (plus Rand Paul) voted for it; most Republicans (plus John Fetterman) voted against; Mark Warner and two others did not vote. The statutory 60-day War Powers clock expires around May 1. Separately, the Senate moved forward on a $70 billion ICE/Border Patrol funding package. Trump told reporters there is “no time frame” on the war and rejected midterm-election political framings; White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said he has “not set a firm deadline to receive an Iranian proposal.” Pakistan’s Pakistan’s mediation bandwidth, which produced the first direct US-Iran face-to-face since 1979 for Round 1, remains the key non-kinetic variable — Islamabad Round 2 collapsed on Apr 22, and no rescheduling has been announced.

Hormuz Escalation: IRGC Seizes MSC Francesca + Epaminondas; Three US CSGs Converging on CENTCOM

The single most consequential kinetic development of Day 54 is the Strait of Hormuz crisis that erupted Wednesday morning, April 22. At approximately 03:38 ET / 05:47 local, UKMTO reported a container ship fired upon without radio warning roughly 15 nautical miles northeast of Oman, with “heavy damage to the bridge” and crew safe. A second vessel was struck about 8 nautical miles off the Iranian coast shortly after. IRGC Navy subsequently confirmed it had seized two container ships — the Panama-flagged MSC Francesca and the Greek-operated Epaminondas — escorting them to Iranian shores and claiming “navigational system tampering.” Both vessels had AIS transponders off at the moment of interception per CBS News analysis. A third vessel, Euphoria, was fired upon and reportedly “stranded on Iranian shores” per semi-official Iranian outlets, though Western sources have not confirmed seizure (flagged UNVERIFIED). Iran’s statement: “Disrupting the order and safety of the Strait of Hormuz is our red line.”

These seizures are the sharpest maritime escalation since the blockade began. They follow the April 20 US Navy action in which USS Spruance (DDG-111) fired on and disabled the Iranian-flagged cargo M/V Touska in the northern Arabian Sea after six hours of warnings; Marines from the 31st MEU rappelled from helicopters (USS Tripoli) and seized it. On April 21, FM Abbas Araghchi called the Touska seizure and the broader blockade “an act of war and a material breach of the ceasefire,” and Iran’s UN Ambassador Iravani filed a formal protest letter with SecGen Guterres. NPR’s Apr 21 tally: 28 commercial vessels turned back since April 13 (up from 27 on Apr 20); ZERO have broken through. IMO SG Arsenio Dominguez said roughly 2,000 vessels remain waiting on both sides. No new UNSC resolution was adopted April 21–22.

Naval posture around the Iran front shows three US carrier strike groups converging on CENTCOM. The USS Abraham Lincoln (CVN-72) / CSG-3 is enforcing the blockade in the northern Arabian Sea with USS Spruance, USS Michael Murphy (DDG-112), and USS Frank E. Petersen Jr. (DDG-121). Defence Security Asia / MizarVizion satellite analysis suggesting Lincoln was repositioned southwest of Oman near Salalah (>1,100 km from the Iranian coast) is flagged UNVERIFIED and not corroborated by USNI. Ford (CVN-78) / CSG-12 transited Suez southbound April 16 and is now operating in the central Red Sea off Jeddah, reportedly pulled south to stay outside Houthi anti-ship missile envelope per MizarVizion (lower-confidence source, flagged). Bush, the U.S. will have three carriers positioned in the Middle East should the ceasefire fail.” The USS Theodore Roosevelt (CVN-71) departed San Diego April 15 with new Raytheon Coyote 8-cell counter-UAS launchers. The USS Boxer (LHD-4) ARG / 11th MEU is transiting the Pacific westbound near Guam. The USS Tripoli (LHA-7) ARG / 31st MEU is in-theater (executed the Touska boarding).
