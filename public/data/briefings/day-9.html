The Yemen Dimension

The US strikes on Houthi positions in Yemen add a fifth theater to the conflict: Iran proper, the Strait of Hormuz, the Lebanon-Israel front, the Iraq-Syria militia zone, and now Yemen. The 12 sites struck across three governorates represent a significant targeting package, but the Houthis have endured years of Saudi-led coalition bombing with limited degradation of capability. Their immediate retaliatory drone launch toward Eilat demonstrated continued operational capacity.

The Yemen front creates a strategic dilemma. Houthi control of the Bab el-Mandeb Strait and Red Sea coastline means they can threaten both east-west shipping (via the Red Sea/Suez route) and north-south shipping (along the Arabian Sea coast). The decision to strike Houthi positions risks escalating their involvement while having limited probability of eliminating their anti-ship capability.

Domestic Opposition Crystallizes

The 500,000-person march in Washington is a lagging indicator — public opinion has been shifting since Day 1, but mass mobilization takes time to organize. The speed of this mobilization (8 days from the first strike to the largest protest since 2003) reflects both the intensity of opposition and the organizing infrastructure inherited from the Gaza-era protest movements. The Minab school imagery has proven as potent as Abu Ghraib was for the Iraq war — a single visual that collapses complex geopolitical arguments into an emotional rejection of the conflict.

The SPR release — 30 million barrels over 60 days — is an economic signaling tool rather than a meaningful supply intervention. At 500,000 barrels per day, it replaces roughly 3% of lost Hormuz throughput. The real message is that the administration recognizes the domestic political vulnerability of rising gas prices and is prepared to use every available lever to moderate them.
