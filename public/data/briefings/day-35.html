The Energy War: $111 Oil and the Inverted Spread

The April 2 oil close may mark a structural turning point in the energy dimension of this conflict. WTI’s settlement at $111.54 — an 11.4% single-session gain — was dramatic enough, but the inversion of the WTI-Brent spread tells a more troubling story. In normal markets, Brent (the international benchmark) trades above WTI (the U.S. benchmark) because of logistical premiums. The reversal — with WTI now above Brent at $109.03 — signals that U.S.-specific supply anxiety has surpassed global concerns. American refiners, dependent on imported crude that must transit either the Strait of Hormuz or alternative routes at massive premium, are bidding up domestic barrels at rates that exceed even the international panic.

The downstream effects are accelerating. Gas reached $4.091 per gallon nationally on April 3 — a $1.11 surge in 35 days, exceeding the steepest climbs after both Hurricane Katrina and the Russia-Ukraine invasion. UAL’s announcement of a 5% capacity cut to offset a 60% jet fuel cost surge since February previews broader economic damage: analysts are now warning that “robust travel demand of 2025 may finally buckle under $5.00-a-gallon gasoline.” The IEA has called this the “largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market,” with Gulf production down approximately 10 million barrels per day. The paradoxical decline in oil majors — XOM down 5.2% and CVX down 4.6% despite surging crude — reflects growing fears that demand destruction at $110+ oil could cap upside even as supply contracts.
