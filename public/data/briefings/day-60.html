Iranian Leadership Fissure Now Overtly Visible: Foreign Ministry Proposal vs. IRGC Telegram Counter-Signal

The defining structural development of Day 59 is that the Iranian leadership’s factional split is no longer subtext — it played out on global wires within hours of itself. The Foreign Ministry, via Pakistani mediators, transmitted a written proposal to the U.S. offering to extend the ceasefire and reopen the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for the U.S. lifting its naval blockade of Iranian ports, and explicitly deferring the nuclear-program file to a later phase. Axios, Bloomberg, AP via Fortune, CNBC, and Al Jazeera’s live blog all confirmed receipt; two regional officials and a U.S. official independently corroborated. WH spokesperson Olivia Wales: “These are sensitive diplomatic discussions and the U.S. will not negotiate through the press… never allowing Iran to have a nuclear weapon.” Trump’s public framing — “within 10 minutes… a new paper that was much better” but “a lot but not enough” — matches the WH Press Sec. Karoline Leavitt confirmation that the National Security Council convened in the Situation Room Apr 27 to review the offer. No public readout was issued by EOD.

And then the IRGC publicly contradicted its own foreign minister. The IRGC Telegram channel declared: “Controlling the Strait of Hormuz and maintaining the shadow of its deterrent effects over America and the White House’s supporters in the region is the definitive strategy of Islamic Iran.” Deputy Parliament Speaker Ali Nikzad, citing an order from Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, told Mehr that Hormuz “under no circumstances” returns to its prior state. Axios’ framing — that the proposal “would remove Trump’s leverage” — likely also describes why the IRGC publicly torpedoed it within hours. Most consequentially, Axios reported that FM Araghchi told Pakistani, Egyptian, Turkish, and Qatari mediators directly: “there’s no consensus inside the Iranian leadership.” That admission is the clearest public signal yet of Tehran’s factional split — and it suggests the proposal’s political viability inside Iran is, at best, contested.

The diplomatic chain is also visibly broadening. Russia entered the frame for the first time in the ceasefire phase: Araghchi met President Putin at the Boris Yeltsin Presidential Library in St Petersburg and FM Lavrov, capping a four-leg shuttle (Pakistan Apr 24 → Oman Apr 26 → Pakistan Apr 26 → Russia Apr 27). Russia’s Vienna envoy Mikhail Ulyanov said the U.S. must abandon “blackmailing” and “ultimatums.” Russia was previously floated as potential repository for Iran’s ~440 kg HEU stockpile; Tehran maintains it will not surrender stockpile. Europe, meanwhile, is publicly impatient: EC President Ursula von der Leyen said it is “too early” to lift Iran sanctions; German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said Iran is “humiliating” the U.S. as peace talks stall. Trump’s posture — cancelling envoy travel, declaring “we have all the cards,” characterizing Iran’s paper as “a lot but not enough,” and refusing to negotiate via press — suggests Washington is using ceasefire-extension drift to pressure factional resolution inside Iran rather than chase an early settlement. The risk is that the May 1 War Powers anniversary forces a timeline before that resolution arrives.
